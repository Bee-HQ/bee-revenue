## ACT 1: THE MURDERS (1:45 - 12:00)

### The Dynasty (1:45 - 4:00)

[MAP-REGION]
**NARRATOR:** The Lowcountry of South Carolina. Stretching from the coast inland through Hampton, Colleton, and Beaufort counties, this region has been dominated by one family for over a century.

[PHOTO-VICTIM] [TEXT-LOWERTHIRD: "Randolph Murdaugh Sr. — 14th Circuit Solicitor, 1920"]
**NARRATOR:** In 1920, Randolph Murdaugh Senior became the elected prosecutor for the 14th Judicial Circuit. The position would stay in the family for the next 86 years.

[PHOTO-COUPLE] [TEXT-LOWERTHIRD: "Three generations of Murdaugh prosecutors"]
**NARRATOR:** His son took over after him. Then his grandson. For three straight generations, a Murdaugh decided who got charged and who walked free in five South Carolina counties. Locals didn't call it the 14th Circuit. They called it Murdaugh Country.

[PHOTO-VICTIM] [TEXT-LOWERTHIRD: "Alex Murdaugh — 4th Generation Lawyer"]
**NARRATOR:** Alex Murdaugh is the fourth generation. Unlike his father and grandfather, Alex didn't become the solicitor. Instead, he joined the family's personal injury law firm, PMPED, where he represented victims of wrongful death and workplace accidents. A respectable career. A beautiful wife named Maggie. Two sons, Buster and Paul. Multiple properties. A seemingly perfect Lowcountry life.

[MAP-AERIAL] [TEXT-LOCATION: "Moselle — 1,770-acre Murdaugh Hunting Estate, Islandton, SC"]
**NARRATOR:** But the crown jewel of the Murdaugh empire is Moselle — a sprawling 1,770-acre hunting estate in rural Islandton. It's here, surrounded by miles of fields and dog kennels, that the Murdaugh story will begin to unravel.

### The Night of June 7, 2021 (4:00 - 8:00)

[BROLL-TIME] [TEXT-TIMELINE: "June 7, 2021 — 8:44 PM"]
**NARRATOR:** It's a Monday evening. Alex, Maggie, and their younger son Paul are all at Moselle. According to Alex, he left the property around 9 PM to visit his ailing mother at her nearby home in Almeda. He says when he returned roughly an hour later, he discovered the unthinkable.

[911-INITIAL]
**NARRATOR:** At 10:07 PM, Colleton County 911 receives a frantic call.

> **ALEX:** "I need the police and an ambulance, 4147 Moselle Road! My wife and my child have been shot badly!"
> **DISPATCHER:** "Are they breathing?"
> **ALEX:** "No ma'am!"
> **DISPATCHER:** "Don't touch them. Don't touch anything. Okay?"
> **ALEX:** "I already touched them trying to... I already tried to turn Paul over."

[BODYCAM-ARRIVE]
**NARRATOR:** Within minutes, Colleton County Sheriff's deputies arrive at the Moselle property. What they find is a scene of devastating violence.

[BODYCAM-EVIDENCE]
**NARRATOR:** Near the dog kennels, officers discover 22-year-old Paul Murdaugh, shot multiple times with a shotgun at close range. The injuries are catastrophic. And just feet away lies his mother, 52-year-old Maggie Murdaugh, shot multiple times with a rifle. She appears to have been trying to run when she was struck. Both are pronounced dead at the scene.

[PHOTO-VICTIM] [TEXT-LOWERTHIRD: "Maggie Murdaugh, 52 — Victim"]
[PHOTO-VICTIM] [TEXT-LOWERTHIRD: "Paul Murdaugh, 22 — Victim"]

[BODYCAM-CONVO]
**NARRATOR:** When deputies approach Alex at the scene, he's visibly distraught. He tells them he had left the property to visit his mother and returned to find his wife and son shot near the kennels. He says he checked for pulses, tried to turn Paul over, but they were both already gone.

**NARRATOR:** In the hours that follow, the South Carolina Law Enforcement Division — SLED — takes over the investigation. Alex Murdaugh is treated as a grieving husband and father. Not a suspect.

**NARRATOR:** But that is about to change.

### Paul's Past — The Boat Crash (8:00 - 12:00)

[TEXT-TIMELINE: "February 24, 2019 — Two Years Earlier"]
[MAP-AERIAL] [TEXT-LOCATION: "Archers Creek Bridge, Beaufort County, SC"]

**NARRATOR:** To understand why someone might have wanted Paul Murdaugh dead, investigators need to look back two years. On a February night in 2019, Paul is behind the wheel of his father's boat after a night of heavy underage drinking. With six people on board, Paul slams the 17-foot boat into a bridge piling at high speed.

[BROLL-DARK]
**NARRATOR:** Five of the six passengers survive with varying injuries. But 19-year-old Mallory Beach is thrown from the boat and disappears into the dark water. Her body won't be found for a week.

[PHOTO-VICTIM] [TEXT-LOWERTHIRD: "Mallory Beach, 19 — Boat Crash Victim"]

**NARRATOR:** Paul is charged with three felonies including boating under the influence causing death. But here's where the Murdaugh name starts to matter. According to survivors, when first responders arrived at the hospital that night, Alex Murdaugh was already there — not comforting victims, but allegedly coaching witnesses. Multiple survivors later claim Alex told them not to talk to investigators and to say Paul wasn't driving.

[TEXT-QUOTE] [BROLL-COURT]
**NARRATOR:** The case against Paul is still pending when he's murdered in June 2021. And the Beach family has filed a civil wrongful death lawsuit against Alex and Maggie Murdaugh personally. A lawsuit that is about to blow the lid off something far bigger than a boat crash.

**NARRATOR:** Because in the process of trying to collect damages from the Murdaughs, the Beach family's attorneys begin looking into the family's finances. And what they find will reveal that Alex Murdaugh has been living a double life for over a decade.

## ACT 2: THE UNRAVELING (12:00 - 28:00)

### The Financial Crimes (12:00 - 18:00)

[BROLL-MONEY] [TEXT-FINANCIAL: "$8,800,000+"]
**NARRATOR:** As the wrongful death lawsuit forces financial scrutiny, investigators discover something staggering. Alex Murdaugh has been systematically stealing money from his own clients for years. Not small amounts. Millions.

[EVIDENCE-FINANCIAL]
**NARRATOR:** The scheme works like this. Alex represents families in wrongful death and injury cases. He wins them large settlements. But instead of sending the money to his clients, he funnels it into a fake bank account called "Forge" — designed to look like a legitimate financial institution called Palmetto State Bank's Forge account, but actually controlled entirely by Alex.

[TEXT-QUOTE]
**NARRATOR:** Among his victims: the family of Gloria Satterfield, the Murdaughs' longtime housekeeper who died after what Alex called a "trip and fall" accident at the Moselle property in 2018. Alex represented the Satterfield family in their own insurance claim, won them $4.3 million — and pocketed every cent. The family received nothing.

[BROLL-COURT]
**NARRATOR:** In total, investigators will eventually identify more than $8.8 million stolen from clients — many of them grieving families who trusted Alex to fight for them. Some estimates push the number above $12 million.

**NARRATOR:** But here's what makes the timing so damning. By June 2021, Alex's financial house of cards is about to collapse. The Beach family lawsuit is exposing his finances. His law firm is asking questions about missing money. He's running out of places to hide. And the two people most connected to his financial exposure — Maggie, who co-owns assets that could be seized in the Beach lawsuit, and Paul, whose boat crash started the chain reaction — are both murdered.

**NARRATOR:** Coincidence? Investigators don't think so.

### The Staged Shooting (18:00 - 22:00)

[TEXT-TIMELINE: "September 4, 2021 — Three Months After the Murders"]

**NARRATOR:** Just three months after the murders, and with SLED investigators quietly looking deeper into his background, something bizarre happens to Alex Murdaugh.

[BODYCAM-ARRIVE]
**NARRATOR:** Alex calls 911 claiming he was shot on the side of a rural road while changing a flat tire. When officers arrive, they find Alex with a superficial head wound, bleeding but alert.

[BODYCAM-CONVO]
**NARRATOR:** Alex tells deputies a truck pulled up, someone fired at him, and drove off. He says he has no idea who would do this or why. But the story immediately raises red flags.

[BROLL-POLICE]
**NARRATOR:** The wound is barely more than a graze. No witnesses see the supposed truck. And the timing — with multiple investigations closing in on Alex — is suspicious at best.

[INTERROGATION]
**NARRATOR:** Within days, the truth comes out. Alex confesses to SLED agents that he arranged the entire thing. He gave a gun to Curtis "Eddie" Smith, a distant cousin and former client, and asked Smith to shoot him in the head so that his surviving son Buster could collect a $10 million life insurance payout. He claims he wanted to end his own life because of an opioid addiction but couldn't do it himself.

[TEXT-QUOTE]
**NARRATOR:** The problem with that story? It doesn't explain why he called 911 immediately after being shot and described a fictitious assailant to officers. This isn't a suicide attempt. It's an insurance fraud scheme.

**NARRATOR:** Alex is charged with insurance fraud, conspiracy, and filing a false police report. He's fired from his own family law firm the same day after the firm discovers he's been stealing client funds. The 100-year-old Murdaugh legal dynasty effectively ends in a single afternoon.

### SLED Closes In (22:00 - 28:00)

[BROLL-POLICE]
**NARRATOR:** With Alex now exposed as a thief and a liar, SLED investigators turn their full attention back to the double murder. And they have something Alex doesn't know about.

[TEXT-QUOTE] [BROLL-DARK]
**NARRATOR:** The kennel video.

[EVIDENCE-DOCUMENT]
**NARRATOR:** At 8:44 PM on the night of the murders, Paul Murdaugh's phone recorded a Snapchat video at the dog kennels. It's just a few seconds long. But in the background, three voices are clearly audible — Paul's, Maggie's, and Alex's. All three are at the kennels together at 8:44 PM.

**NARRATOR:** But Alex told investigators he was nowhere near the kennels that evening. He claimed he was at the main house, then left to visit his mother, and only discovered the bodies when he returned. The kennel video proves Alex lied about his alibi. He was at the scene with his wife and son minutes before they were killed.

[INTERROGATION]
**NARRATOR:** When SLED agents confront Alex about the video, he doubles down. He insists the voices don't prove he was at the kennels at the time of the killings. He maintains his timeline. But the damage is done.

[TEXT-TIMELINE: "July 14, 2022"]
**NARRATOR:** On July 14, 2022, Alex Murdaugh is formally indicted on two counts of murder and two counts of possession of a weapon during the commission of a violent crime for the deaths of Maggie and Paul Murdaugh.

**NARRATOR:** The trial is set for the following January. And it's about to become the most watched criminal trial in South Carolina history.

## ACT 3: THE TRIAL (29:30 - 45:00)

### Opening Statements (29:30 - 32:00)

[TEXT-TIMELINE: "January 25, 2023 — Colleton County Courthouse"]
[MAP-AERIAL] [TEXT-LOCATION: "Walterboro, South Carolina"]
[TRIAL-COURTROOM]

**NARRATOR:** The trial of Alex Murdaugh begins in the small courthouse in Walterboro, South Carolina. With cameras allowed in the courtroom, millions will watch the proceedings live. Judge Clifton Newman presides.

[TRIAL-TESTIMONY]
**NARRATOR:** Lead prosecutor Creighton Waters lays out the state's case in a devastating opening statement. He tells the jury that Alex Murdaugh killed his wife and son at the dog kennels using two different weapons — a family shotgun for Paul and a .300 Blackout rifle for Maggie. He describes how Alex then spent the next 45 minutes creating an alibi before calling 911.

[TRIAL-TESTIMONY]
**NARRATOR:** Waters emphasizes the kennel video as the centerpiece of the prosecution's case. He tells the jury that once they hear Alex's voice in that video, they'll know he lied about everything.

[TRIAL-TESTIMONY]
**NARRATOR:** Defense attorney Dick Harpootlian counters by arguing that SLED botched the investigation, failed to properly secure the crime scene, and never tested Alex for gunshot residue. He suggests the murders could have been committed by anyone — perhaps someone connected to the boat crash or Alex's financial dealings seeking revenge.

### The Evidence (32:00 - 38:00)

[TRIAL-TESTIMONY]
**NARRATOR:** Over the next five weeks, the prosecution calls more than 60 witnesses. The evidence mounts piece by piece.

[TRIAL-EXHIBIT]
**NARRATOR:** First, cell phone data. Alex's phone showed no activity between 8:09 PM and 9:02 PM on the night of the murders — a gap investigators say is consistent with him turning the phone off or leaving it behind while committing the killings. Paul's phone, recovered at the scene, shows the kennel video was recorded at 8:44 PM. At 8:49 PM, Paul's phone locks and never unlocks again. That five-minute window is when prosecutors believe the murders occurred.

[TRIAL-EXHIBIT]
**NARRATOR:** Second, OnStar data from Alex's Chevrolet Suburban shows the vehicle made a rapid trip from the kennels to the main house around 9 PM — a trip prosecutors say was Alex rushing to clean up and establish his alibi before driving to his mother's house.

[TRIAL-EXHIBIT] [EVIDENCE-PHYSICAL]
**NARRATOR:** Third, a blue rain jacket. Found in the home, the jacket tests positive for Maggie's blood on the inside and contains 52 particles of gunshot residue. Prosecutors argue Alex wore it during the shootings, then shoved it in a closet.

[TRIAL-TESTIMONY]
**NARRATOR:** And then, one by one, witnesses from Alex's life take the stand to describe the financial desperation he was under. His law firm's CFO. His paralegal. Former clients who never received their settlements. Each testimony adds another layer to the motive: Alex was cornered, and murder was his way out.

### The Kennel Video (38:00 - 40:00)

[TRIAL-EXHIBIT]
**NARRATOR:** But the moment that changes everything comes when prosecutors play the kennel video for the jury.

**NARRATOR:** The video is short — just a few seconds of Paul filming his dog at the kennels. But in the background, the voices are unmistakable. Witness after witness — friends, colleagues, family members — takes the stand and confirms: that is Alex's voice, Maggie's voice, and Paul's voice. All three together at the kennels at 8:44 PM.

[TEXT-QUOTE]
**NARRATOR:** One of the most powerful witnesses is Rogan Gibson, Paul's close friend, who had been texting Paul about a dog in the kennel. Gibson identifies all three voices without hesitation. He knows them like family.

**NARRATOR:** The kennel video doesn't just place Alex at the scene. It destroys his alibi completely. And it presents the jury with a simple question: if Alex was at the kennels with his wife and son minutes before they were killed, and he lied about being there, why?

### Alex Takes the Stand (40:00 - 44:00)

[TRIAL-TESTIMONY]
**NARRATOR:** In a move that stuns legal observers, defense attorneys put Alex Murdaugh on the witness stand. It's a massive gamble. And it's about to backfire.

**NARRATOR:** Under questioning from his own lawyer, Alex admits — for the first time — that he lied to SLED investigators about being at the kennels that night. He says he was there, heard the kennel video, recognized his own voice, but lied about it because of his opioid-addled state of mind and what he calls "paranoid thinking" about law enforcement.

[TEXT-QUOTE]
> **ALEX (on the stand):** "I lied about going down to the kennels. And I'm so sorry that I did."

**NARRATOR:** But when prosecutor Creighton Waters begins his cross-examination, the courtroom shifts. Waters walks Alex through lie after lie — to investigators, to his family, to his clients, to his law partners. Each one Alex admits to, trying to distinguish between his lies about the kennels and what he insists is the truth about his innocence.

[TRIAL-TESTIMONY]
**NARRATOR:** Then Waters asks about Gloria Satterfield — the housekeeper whose death Alex allegedly profited from. And Alex breaks.

[TEXT-QUOTE]
> **ALEX (crying):** "I did him so bad... I took his money that he didn't deserve — that he deserved. And I'm so sorry."

**NARRATOR:** It's unclear whether he says "I did him so bad" referring to a client or something else entirely. But the damage is done. A man who has spent two weeks maintaining his innocence has just demonstrated on live television that he is capable of looking people in the eye and lying about the most consequential things imaginable.

### The Verdict (44:00 - 45:00)

[TEXT-TIMELINE: "March 2, 2023"]
[TRIAL-COURTROOM]

**NARRATOR:** After six weeks of testimony and less than three hours of deliberation, the jury returns its verdict.

[TRIAL-VERDICT]
> **CLERK:** "On the charge of murder in the death of Margaret Kennedy Branstetter Murdaugh... guilty. On the charge of murder in the death of Paul Terry Murdaugh... guilty."

[TRIAL-COURTROOM]
**NARRATOR:** Alex shows no visible reaction. The courtroom erupts in murmurs.

**NARRATOR:** The next day, Judge Clifton Newman delivers the sentence.

[TRIAL-VERDICT]
> **JUDGE NEWMAN:** "I sentence you to the South Carolina Department of Corrections for the rest of your natural life. Life in prison without the possibility of parole."

## ACT 4: THE AFTERMATH (45:00 - 52:00)

### The Full Scope (45:00 - 48:00)

[BROLL-COURT]
**NARRATOR:** But the murder conviction is only the beginning. In the months that follow, the full scope of Alex Murdaugh's crimes comes into devastating focus.

[TEXT-FINANCIAL: "$8,800,000+ stolen from clients"]
**NARRATOR:** In November 2023, Alex pleads guilty to 22 counts of state financial crimes — fraud, money laundering, breach of trust, and computer crimes — stealing over $8.8 million from his clients over a decade. He receives 27 years, to run concurrent with his life sentence.

[TEXT-FINANCIAL: "Federal charges — 22 counts"]
**NARRATOR:** Then in 2024, federal charges follow. Alex pleads guilty to wire fraud, bank fraud, and money laundering. The federal judge sentences him to 40 years — to run consecutive to his state sentences.

[BROLL-DARK]
**NARRATOR:** The Murdaugh law firm, PMPED, renamed itself. The 14th Circuit Solicitor's office, held by the Murdaugh family for 86 years, moved on long ago. The dynasty that shaped South Carolina's Lowcountry for over a century was dismantled by the very man who was supposed to carry it forward.

### The Web of Deaths (48:00 - 50:00)

[BROLL-DARK] [PHOTO-VICTIM]
**NARRATOR:** And then there are the other deaths. Stephen Smith, a 19-year-old found dead on a road near Murdaugh property in 2015. His case was reopened after the murders. Gloria Satterfield, the housekeeper who died after a "trip and fall" at Moselle in 2018 — with Alex stealing her death settlement. Whether Alex's crimes extend beyond Maggie and Paul remains an open question.

### Current Status (50:00 - 52:00)

[BROLL-COURT] [TEXT-STATUS]
**NARRATOR:** As of March 2026, Alex Murdaugh's murder conviction is under appeal. His defense argues that the clerk of court, Becky Hill, tampered with the jury during deliberations and that her actions warrant a new trial. The South Carolina Supreme Court heard oral arguments on February 11, 2026, and a ruling is pending. If the conviction is overturned, Alex would face a retrial.

[PHOTO-MUGSHOT] [TEXT-STATUS: "Murder — Life without parole | State financial crimes — 27 years | Federal financial crimes — 40 years"]
**NARRATOR:** Alex Murdaugh currently sits in a federal prison, where he will remain regardless of the murder appeal outcome. He is 57 years old. His combined sentences ensure he will die behind bars.

**NARRATOR:** He maintains his innocence in the murders of his wife and son. He remains guilty on all other charges. And the Murdaugh name — once the most powerful in South Carolina's Lowcountry — is now synonymous with something else entirely.

[BROLL-DARK]

**NARRATOR:** Maggie Murdaugh was 52. Paul Murdaugh was 22.

## PRODUCTION NOTES

### Footage Advantage
This case is exceptional for footage availability. The entire trial was televised on Court TV. The 911 call, bodycam, and SLED interrogations are all public. The kennel video was played in open court. This means the "must source" category is almost entirely already available online — minimal FOIA requests needed.

### Narrative Choice
The screenplay focuses on the murder investigation and trial (June 2021 → March 2023) with the dynasty/boat crash/financial crimes as context. A potential Part 2 could deep-dive into the financial crimes or the other suspicious deaths (Stephen Smith, Gloria Satterfield).

### Sponsor Placement
Placed at minute 28 (54% of runtime) — after the murder investigation and financial crimes are established, right before the trial begins. Maximum tension: viewer knows Alex is a liar and thief, now wants to see the trial. Won't leave through the ad.
