## ACT 1: THE DYNASTY AND THE DEAD (2:30 - 17:00)

### Murdaugh Country (2:30 - 5:00)

[MAP-TACTICAL] [SPLIT-INFO: "The Lowcountry — Hampton, Colleton, Beaufort Counties, South Carolina"]
**NARRATOR:** The Lowcountry of South Carolina. Stretching from the coast inland through five rural counties, this region operates at a pace that hasn't changed much in a hundred years. And for a hundred of those years, one family controlled who went to prison and who walked free.

[PIP-SINGLE] [LOWER-THIRD: "Randolph Murdaugh Sr. — 14th Circuit Solicitor, 1920"]
**NARRATOR:** In 1920, Randolph Murdaugh Senior is elected as the solicitor — the top prosecutor — for the 14th Judicial Circuit. It's a position with enormous power. The solicitor decides which cases to bring to trial and which ones to drop. And the Murdaugh family will hold that seat for the next eighty-six years.

[PIP-SINGLE] [LOWER-THIRD: "Three Generations of Murdaugh Prosecutors — 1920 to 2006"]
**NARRATOR:** His son takes over. Then his grandson. Three straight generations of Murdaugh prosecutors, deciding the fate of everyone in five South Carolina counties. Locals don't call it the 14th Circuit. They call it Murdaugh Country.

[PIP-SINGLE] [LOWER-THIRD: "Alex Murdaugh — 4th Generation, Peters Murdaugh Parker Eltzroth & Detrick (PMPED)"]
**NARRATOR:** Alex Murdaugh is the fourth generation. Born in 1968, he graduates from the University of South Carolina School of Law and joins the family firm, PMPED. Unlike his father and grandfather, Alex doesn't become the solicitor. Instead, he handles personal injury cases — representing families who've lost loved ones in wrongful deaths and workplace accidents. He also volunteers part-time in the solicitor's office. The Murdaugh name opens every door.

[COURTROOM: opening statement — Waters]
>> **CREIGHTON WATERS (trial opening statement):** "The Murdaugh name carries a lot of weight in the Lowcountry. A lot of weight."

**NARRATOR:** That's lead prosecutor Creighton Waters. He will later argue that this weight — this century of unchecked power — is exactly what made Alex Murdaugh believe he could get away with anything.

### Maggie and Paul — The People We Lose (5:00 - 7:00)

[PIP-SINGLE] [LOWER-THIRD: "Margaret 'Maggie' Murdaugh — Wife, Mother"]
**NARRATOR:** Maggie Murdaugh is Alex's wife. She grew up in the Lowcountry, married into the dynasty, and by all accounts is a warm, present mother who pours herself into her sons' lives. She trusts Alex. She trusts the family name. She has no idea what her husband is doing behind closed doors.

**NARRATOR:** But in the months before her death, that trust begins to crack. Listen to what Maggie tells Blanca — the words of a woman who senses something is deeply wrong but can't yet see the shape of it.

[COURTROOM: testimony — Blanca]

**NARRATOR:** That's Blanca, the Murdaugh family's longtime housekeeper. She will later testify that in the months before her death, Maggie confides she is terrified about a thirty-million-dollar lawsuit hanging over the family. [PIP-SINGLE] [LOWER-THIRD: "Paul Murdaugh — 22 Years Old"]
**NARRATOR:** Paul Murdaugh is Alex and Maggie's younger son. Twenty-two years old. He loves the Lowcountry life — hunting, fishing, the dogs at the Moselle kennels. His best friend, Rogan Gibson, describes him as loyal and fun-loving.

[DOCUMENT-MOCKUP]

**NARRATOR:** That's Paul's voice. Recorded at 8:44 PM on June 7, 2021. The last thing he ever films — a throwaway video of a dog at the kennels. Something that will soon become the most important fifty seconds in South Carolina legal history.

**NARRATOR:** Her body is found five miles downriver a week later. Paul is charged with boating under the influence causing death. Needless to say, for a family that has controlled the local justice system for a century, having a son charged with killing a teenager is a catastrophic problem.

**NARRATOR:** But what they don't realize is how much worse it's about to get. Because the Beach family files a wrongful death lawsuit against Alex and Maggie Murdaugh personally. And that lawsuit is about to force open Alex Murdaugh's finances. Something he cannot allow.

**NARRATOR:** What Alex doesn't tell the CFO is that the seven hundred and ninety-two thousand is only the tip of the iceberg. **NARRATOR:** Now the walls are closing in. The Beach lawsuit is prying open his books. His own law firm is asking questions. His financial empire is a house of cards, and it's about to collapse.

### Maggie Comes to Moselle (12:30 - 14:00)

[MAP-3D] [SPLIT-INFO: "Moselle — 1,772-acre Murdaugh Hunting Estate, Islandton, SC"]
**NARRATOR:** Maggie is reluctant. She doesn't want to go. But Alex insists. He tells her his father, Randolph the Third, is dying — that she needs to come visit. So she drives out to the sprawling estate.

**NARRATOR:** Why does Alex insist Maggie come to Moselle that night? This is a question investigators will ask themselves for the next two years. And the answer they arrive at is devastating.

**NARRATOR:** Paul is already at the property. He's down at the dog kennels. At some point in the evening, the three of them — Alex, Maggie, and Paul — are together at the kennels. And something terrible is about to happen.

### The Kennel Video (14:00 - 15:30)

[DOCUMENT-MOCKUP] [TIMELINE-MARKER: "8:44 PM — Paul's Kennel Video"]
**NARRATOR:** At 8:44 PM, Paul records a video on his phone at the dog kennels. He's filming one of the dogs — a cock-a-poo named Bubba who has a hurt tail. The video is about fifty seconds long. It's mundane. Everyday.

**NARRATOR:** But in the background, three voices are clearly audible. Paul's. Maggie's. And Alex's. All three are at the kennels together at 8:44 PM.

**NARRATOR:** This video will never send. Paul tries to upload it on Snapchat, but the connection fails. The video sits locked inside his phone, protected by a six-digit passcode — 041499. Paul's birthday. April 14, 1999.

**NARRATOR:** At 8:49 PM — just five minutes after the kennel video — Paul's phone locks. And it never unlocks again. Paul Murdaugh is dead.

**NARRATOR:** What happened between 8:49 PM and 10:07 PM? Where are the murder weapons? And what will the jury decide when they finally hear this video almost two years later? Things are about to take a turn.

### The Murders (15:30 - 17:00)

[BROLL-DARK: atmospheric — night/rural] [TIMELINE-MARKER: "Between 8:49 PM and approximately 9:06 PM"]
**NARRATOR:** Near the dog kennels at Moselle, Paul is shot twice with a twelve-gauge shotgun at close range. His mother Maggie is shot five times with a .300 Blackout semi-automatic rifle. Both weapons belong to the Murdaugh family.

**NARRATOR:** Maggie appears to have been trying to run when she was struck. A detail that's highly unusual — she didn't run toward the house for safety. She ran away from the kennels, away from whoever was shooting. As if the threat was coming from someone already close. Someone she knew. The woman who told Blanca she'd give everything to make it go away spent her final moments running from the one person she trusted most.

**NARRATOR:** Neither the shotgun nor the rifle is at the scene. Two family-owned weapons, used to murder two family members, have vanished from a 1,770-acre property. Where are the murder weapons? Over the next two years, investigators will tear apart every inch of Moselle looking for them.

**NARRATOR:** And then, roughly an hour later, Alex Murdaugh picks up his phone.

## ACT 2: THE UNRAVELING (17:00 - 32:00)

### The 911 Call (17:00 - 19:00)

[WAVEFORM-AERIAL] [TIMELINE-MARKER: "10:07 PM — Alex Calls 911"]
**NARRATOR:** At 10:07 PM — seventy-eight minutes after Paul's phone went dark — Alex dials 911 from Moselle.

>> **ALEX (911 call):** "I need the police and an ambulance! 4147 Moselle Road!"
>> **DISPATCHER:** "Okay, what's going on?"
>> **ALEX:** "My wife and my child have been shot badly!"
>> **DISPATCHER:** "Are they breathing?"
>> **ALEX:** "No ma'am!"
>> **DISPATCHER:** "Don't touch them. Don't touch anything, okay?"
>> **ALEX:** "I already touched them trying to... I already tried to turn Paul over."
>> **DISPATCHER:** "Did you hear anything? Did you come home and find them?"
>> **ALEX:** "I've been gone. I just came back."

[BODYCAM: Deputy Greene arrival at Moselle]
**NARRATOR:** Within minutes, Colleton County deputies arrive at Moselle. Deputy Daniel Greene's bodycam captures the scene.

>> **DEPUTY GREENE (bodycam):** "What do we got?"

**NARRATOR:** What they find is devastating. Paul lies near the kennels, killed by shotgun blasts at close range. Feet away, Maggie lies in the grass, shot five times with a rifle. Both are pronounced dead at the scene.

### The First Interview — David Owen's Patrol Car (19:00 - 21:30)

[INTERROGATION] [TIMELINE-MARKER: "Night of June 7, 2021 — SLED Takes Over"]
**NARRATOR:** The South Carolina Law Enforcement Division — SLED — takes over the investigation within hours. Special Agent David Owen conducts Alex's first interview in the back of his patrol car. Alex is treated as a grieving husband and father. Not a suspect.

>> **DAVID OWEN:** "Alex, walk me through your evening. Where were you tonight?"
>> **ALEX:** "I was at the house. Took a nap. I drove over to see my mama at Almeda — she's got Alzheimer's. I was there thirty, forty minutes. Came back and found them down there."
>> **OWEN:** "Did you go down to the kennels at all this evening?"
>> **ALEX:** "No. I didn't go down there."

**NARRATOR:** "I didn't go down there." It's a simple, definitive statement. And it is a lie. Because Alex's voice is on Paul's kennel video at 8:44 PM. He was at the kennels. He was with his wife and son minutes before they died.

**NARRATOR:** But investigators don't know about the kennel video yet. Paul's phone is locked. The passcode is his birthday — 041499 — but it will take months for digital forensics to crack it. And until they do, Alex's lie holds.

### Three Days Later — "I Did Him So Bad" (21:30 - 23:30)

[TIMELINE-MARKER: "June 10, 2021 — Three Days After the Murders"]
**NARRATOR:** Three days after the murders, Alex's father — Randolph Murdaugh the Third, the former solicitor — dies at age 81. The patriarch of the dynasty is gone. The family is reeling.

[INTERROGATION]
**NARRATOR:** That same day, SLED agents interview Alex again at his brother John Marvin's property. During the interview, agents show Alex photos and video of Paul's body. And it's at this point that something happens that will echo through the entire trial.

>> **SLED SPECIAL AGENT JEFF CROFT (trial testimony):** "I heard Mr. Murdaugh say, 'I did him so bad.'"

**NARRATOR:** "I did him so bad." SLED Agent Jeff Croft is certain of what he heard. But the defense will insist Alex actually said "They did him so bad" — an expression of grief, not a confession. Alex's surviving son Buster and his brother John Marvin both testify that Alex said "they." Not "I."

**NARRATOR:** The audio quality is ambiguous enough for both interpretations. But for investigators, the statement is unsettling. A detail they won't forget.

### The Financial House of Cards (23:30 - 26:00)

[BROLL-DARK: financial — money/documents] [FINANCIAL-CARD: "$12,000,000+ — Stolen Over a Decade"]
**NARRATOR:** In the weeks and months after the murders, as SLED investigates the killings, another investigation is running in parallel. The Beach family's wrongful death lawsuit — the one triggered by Paul's boat crash — is forcing open the Murdaugh finances. And what emerges is staggering.

[FINANCIAL-CARD]
**NARRATOR:** Alex Murdaugh has been stealing from his own clients for over a decade. Not small amounts. Millions. The scheme works like this: Alex represents families in wrongful death cases. He wins them settlements. But instead of sending the money to his clients, he funnels it into a fake bank account he calls "Forge" — designed to look like the legitimate settlement company Forge Consulting, but controlled entirely by Alex.

**NARRATOR:** He steals from the Satterfield family. He steals from accident victims. He steals from disabled clients. He steals from people who came to him at the worst moments of their lives and trusted the Murdaugh name to fight for them. Total estimated theft: over twelve million dollars.

**NARRATOR:** He also funnels roughly $2.4 million to his drug dealer, Curtis Eddie Smith, through 437 separate checks over eight years. Alex has a crushing opioid addiction. And now, with the CFO confrontation on June 7, his law firm knows money is missing.

**NARRATOR:** It's at this point that investigators begin connecting the financial timeline to the murders. The CFO confronts Alex about $792,000 on the morning of June 7. That evening, Alex's wife and son are dead. Coincidence? Investigators don't think so.

### The Staged Shooting (26:00 - 28:30)

[TIMELINE-MARKER: "September 4, 2021 — Three Months After the Murders"]
**NARRATOR:** Three months pass. And then something bizarre happens. On September 4, Alex calls 911 claiming he was shot on the side of a rural road while changing a tire.

[WAVEFORM-AERIAL]
>> **ALEX (roadside shooting 911 call):** "Somebody just shot me! I'm on Old Salkehatchie Road..."

[BODYCAM: roadside shooting response]
**NARRATOR:** When officers arrive, they find Alex with a superficial head wound. Barely more than a graze. He tells them a truck pulled up, someone fired, and drove off. No witnesses. No truck. And the timing — with multiple investigations closing in — is suspicious at best.

[INTERROGATION]
**NARRATOR:** Within days, the truth comes out. Alex confesses to SLED agents that he arranged the entire thing. He gave a gun to Curtis Eddie Smith — his drug dealer and distant cousin — and told Smith to shoot him in the head so that his surviving son Buster could collect a ten-million-dollar life insurance payout.

**NARRATOR:** The problem? Alex called 911 immediately after being shot and described a fictitious assailant. This isn't a suicide attempt. It's insurance fraud.

**NARRATOR:** Alex is fired from his own law firm the same day. The hundred-year Murdaugh legal dynasty effectively ends in a single afternoon. He checks himself into a rehab facility in Florida.

### Arrested at Rehab (28:30 - 30:00)

[TIMELINE-MARKER: "October 14, 2021 — Evolve Recovery Center, Florida"]
[BODYCAM: arrest at rehab facility]
**NARRATOR:** On October 14, deputies from the Orange County Florida Sheriff's Office arrive at Evolve Recovery Center with two felony warrants for the stolen Satterfield insurance money.

>> **DEPUTY (arrest bodycam):** "Alex Murdaugh? We have a warrant for your arrest."

**NARRATOR:** Things are about to escalate. Because while Alex sits in a jail cell on fraud charges, SLED investigators have finally cracked Paul's phone.

### The Phone Unlocks (30:00 - 32:00)

[DOCUMENT-MOCKUP] [QUOTE-CARD: "Passcode: 041499 — Paul's Birthday"]
**NARRATOR:** For months, Paul Murdaugh's iPhone has sat in an evidence locker, locked. Digital forensics specialists have been working to access it. And when they finally crack the six-digit passcode — 041499, Paul's birthday, April 14, 1999 — they find the video.

**NARRATOR:** Fifty seconds. Shot at 8:44 PM on June 7, 2021. Paul filming a dog at the kennels. And in the background — three voices. Paul. Maggie. And Alex.

**NARRATOR:** Alex, who told Agent David Owen he never went to the kennels that night. Alex, who maintained his timeline through every interview. Alex, who swore he left the main house, drove to his mother's, and came back to find the bodies.

**NARRATOR:** His own dead son's phone just proved he lied about everything.

[INTERROGATION]
**NARRATOR:** When SLED agents confront Alex about the video in the August 11 interview, he doubles down.

## ACT 3: THE TRIAL (33:30 - 50:00)

### Opening Day (33:30 - 35:30)

[TIMELINE-MARKER: "January 25, 2023 — Colleton County Courthouse, Walterboro, SC"]
[MAP-3D] [SPLIT-INFO: "Walterboro, South Carolina"]
[COURTROOM: wide — courtroom establishing]
**NARRATOR:** The trial of Alex Murdaugh begins in the small courthouse in Walterboro, South Carolina — the same judicial circuit his family dominated for eighty-six years. With cameras allowed in the courtroom, millions will watch the proceedings live. Judge Clifton Newman presides.

[COURTROOM: opening statement — Waters]
>> **CREIGHTON WATERS (opening statement):** "The evidence will show that Alex Murdaugh killed his wife, Maggie, and his son, Paul, on the evening of June 7, 2021, at the dog kennels on their property known as Moselle."

**NARRATOR:** Lead prosecutor Creighton Waters lays it out plainly. Two different weapons — a family shotgun for Paul and a .300 Blackout rifle for Maggie. Both weapons belonging to the family. Neither one ever recovered. And a kennel video that proves the defendant lied about his alibi.

[COURTROOM: opening statement — Harpootlian]
>> **DICK HARPOOTLIAN (defense opening):** "SLED botched this investigation from the beginning. They rushed to judgment. They never tested Alex for gunshot residue."

**NARRATOR:** Defense attorney Dick Harpootlian — a high-profile South Carolina lawyer and state senator — counters that SLED failed to properly secure the crime scene, never tested Alex for gunshot residue, and rushed to blame the man closest to the victims. He suggests the real killers could be anyone connected to the boat crash or Alex's financial dealings.

### Blanca's Testimony — The Clothes (35:30 - 38:00)

[COURTROOM: testimony — Blanca clothing] [LOWER-THIRD: "Blanca Turrubiate-Simpson — Murdaugh Family Housekeeper"]
**NARRATOR:** Over the next five weeks, the prosecution calls more than sixty witnesses. But some testimonies land harder than others. And one of the most devastating comes from Blanca — the woman who cleaned the Murdaugh home and saw what no one else did.

>> **BLANCA (trial testimony):** "That morning, Mr. Alex was wearing a seafoam green polo shirt."

**NARRATOR:** A seafoam green polo. Blanca remembers this detail because she sees Alex in it the morning of June 7. But later that day, in a Snapchat video, Alex is wearing a different shirt. And the clothes Alex was wearing at the time of the kennel video — the clothes from 8:44 PM — are never found.

>> **BLANCA (trial testimony):** "The next morning when I came to the house, the shower in Mr. Alex's room — it was wet. There was a towel on the floor. And there were khaki pants just left there on the floor."

**NARRATOR:** A wet shower. A wet towel. Pants on the floor. The morning after his wife and son were murdered, the house tells a story Alex doesn't want anyone to hear. He changed clothes. He showered. He cleaned up.

>> **BLANCA (trial testimony):** "Mrs. Maggie was worried. She told me Alex was not being truthful about the finances."

**NARRATOR:** Blanca also testifies that Maggie confided in her about the family's money problems — that Maggie was terrified about the thirty-million-dollar lawsuit from the Beach family. That she suspected Alex was hiding something. Maggie Murdaugh spent the final months of her life worried that her husband was lying to her. She was right.

### Shelly Smith — The Alibi Problem (38:00 - 39:30)

[COURTROOM: testimony — Shelly Smith] [LOWER-THIRD: "Mushelle 'Shelly' Smith — Caregiver for Alex's Mother"]
**NARRATOR:** The prosecution then calls Shelly Smith — the nighttime caregiver for Alex's mother, who has Alzheimer's. On the night of the murders, Alex drove to his mother's house in Almeda and claims he was there for thirty to forty minutes. But Shelly tells a different story.

>> **SHELLY SMITH (trial testimony, paraphrased narration):** Alex asked her to tell investigators he was at his mother's house for thirty to forty minutes.

**NARRATOR:** Alex asked Shelly to lie about how long he was there. The reality, backed by OnStar data from his Chevrolet Suburban, is that he stayed roughly twenty minutes. And during that visit, his vehicle drove around to outbuildings near the wood line behind the house — a route that makes no sense for a normal visit to a sick mother.

**NARRATOR:** Every piece of evidence is pulling in the same direction. But the moment that changes everything is still coming.

### The Kennel Video in Court (39:30 - 42:00)

[COURTROOM: exhibit — kennel video played] [TIMELINE-MARKER: "Day 8 of Trial — Approximately February 2, 2023"]
**NARRATOR:** When prosecutors play the kennel video for the jury, the courtroom shifts. This is the moment the entire trial hinges on.

**NARRATOR:** The video is short — fifty seconds of Paul filming a dog named Bubba at the kennels. But in the background, the voices are unmistakable. And one by one, witnesses take the stand to confirm what the jury can already hear.

[COURTROOM: testimony — Rogan Gibson kennel video ID]
>> **ROGAN GIBSON (Paul's best friend, testimony):** "That is Alex, Maggie, and Paul. I am one hundred percent sure."

**NARRATOR:** Rogan Gibson knows these voices like family. He'd been texting with Paul about a dog in the kennel that very evening. He identifies all three voices without hesitation.

**NARRATOR:** As the video plays, Alex Murdaugh sits in the defendant's chair and sobs. For almost two years, he maintained he was never at the kennels. Now, his dead son's phone is calling him a liar in open court.

**NARRATOR:** Paul Murdaugh testified from the grave. The last thing he ever filmed convicted his own father.

**NARRATOR:** But here's the question hanging over the courtroom: if Alex was at the kennels with his wife and son at 8:44 PM, and both were dead by 8:49 PM, and Alex lied to everyone about being there — why? What is his explanation?

### Alex Takes the Stand (42:00 - 44:00)

[COURTROOM: testimony — Alex takes the stand] [TIMELINE-MARKER: "February 23-24, 2023"]
**NARRATOR:** In a move that stuns legal observers, the defense puts Alex Murdaugh on the witness stand. It's a massive gamble. And it begins with a bombshell.

>> **ALEX (direct examination):** "I lied about going down to the kennels. And I'm so sorry that I did."

**NARRATOR:** Within moments of taking the stand, Alex admits what he denied for almost two years. He was at the kennels. He heard the video. He recognized his own voice. But he lied because — he says — his opioid addiction caused "paranoid thinking" about law enforcement.

>> **ALEX (direct examination):** "I did not kill Maggie. I did not kill Paul. I would never hurt Maggie and I would never hurt Paul."

**NARRATOR:** He's emotional. He cries. He admits to stealing from clients, to the insurance fraud scheme, to lying to his partners, his family, and his friends. But he draws the line at murder. He insists he is not a "family annihilator."

**NARRATOR:** The defense rests its case on one bet: that Alex Murdaugh can look twelve jurors in the eye and convince them he's telling the truth this time. But he has no idea what's about to happen next.

### The Creighton Waters Cross-Examination (44:00 - 50:00)

[COURTROOM: cross-examination — Waters vs Alex] [LOWER-THIRD: "Creighton Waters — SC Senior Assistant Deputy Attorney General"]
**NARRATOR:** When lead prosecutor Creighton Waters stands up for cross-examination, the temperature in the courtroom drops. This is the confrontation the entire trial has been building toward. And for the next several hours, Waters methodically dismantles Alex Murdaugh piece by piece.

>> **WATERS:** "Mr. Murdaugh, you would agree that you are a very good liar?"
>> **ALEX:** "I've lied well."

**NARRATOR:** It starts with the lies. Waters walks Alex through every deception — to investigators, to his law partners, to his clients, to his own family. Each time, Alex admits the lie. Each time, he insists the murders are different.

>> **WATERS:** "You lied to SLED about the kennels."
>> **ALEX:** "Yes sir."
>> **WATERS:** "You lied to your law partners about millions of dollars."
>> **ALEX:** "Yes sir."

>> **ALEX:** "Yes sir. I'm ashamed."

**NARRATOR:** Waters then turns to the timeline. He walks Alex through every minute of the evening — the kennel video at 8:44, Paul's phone going dark at 8:49, the gap of no activity on Alex's phone, the rapid vehicle movements captured by OnStar, the short visit to his mother's house.

>> **WATERS:** "Between 8:49 PM and 10:07 PM — that's the missing hour, Mr. Murdaugh. What were you doing?"
>> **ALEX:** "I told you. I went to my mama's house."

**NARRATOR:** Then Waters zeroes in on the kennel video.

>> **WATERS:** "You told Agent Owen you never went to the kennels. You told them multiple times. And the only reason you're sitting here today admitting it is because your own son's video caught you in a lie."
>> **ALEX:** "I'm not proud of that."
>> **WATERS:** "Exposed as a liar by your own son — by Paul."

**NARRATOR:** The courtroom is silent. Alex shifts in his chair. And then Waters goes to the financial crimes — systematically walking through the Satterfield theft, the Forge account, the lies to clients.

>> **WATERS:** "You stole $4.3 million from the sons of your dead housekeeper and they didn't know it."
>> **ALEX (breaking down):** "I did him so bad..."

[QUOTE-CARD: "'I did him so bad' — Alex Murdaugh, on the stand"]
**NARRATOR:** "I did him so bad." This time, there's no ambiguity about context. Alex is talking about one of the Satterfield sons. But for investigators sitting in the courtroom — the same investigators who heard a similar phrase on June 10, 2021, when Alex was viewing Paul's body — the echo is devastating.

**NARRATOR:** It's at this point that the defense's strategy collapses. Alex has spent two weeks insisting he's innocent. But now, on live television, he has demonstrated that he can look anyone in the eye — investigators, judges, his own family — and lie about the most consequential things imaginable. If he can steal millions from the family of his dead housekeeper and lie about it for years, what else is he capable of?

>> **WATERS:** "Mr. Murdaugh, isn't it true that the only person who had the motive, the means, and the opportunity to murder Maggie and Paul... was you?"
>> **ALEX:** "I did not kill my wife and my son."

**NARRATOR:** The jury will decide who they believe. And they won't take long.
