# Dr Insanity -- Narration DNA & Style Guide

> Derived from analysis of 17 transcripts (~721K characters total) from the @DrInsanityCrime YouTube channel. Every pattern cited below appears across multiple videos unless noted otherwise.

---

## 1. Opening / Cold Open Patterns

### 1.1 Structure: The "Flash-Forward Montage"

Every Dr Insanity video opens with a **cold open montage** -- a rapid-fire sequence of real audio clips (911 calls, bodycam, interrogation) that previews the most dramatic moments of the case. This montage runs **30-90 seconds** and typically contains **3-7 distinct audio clips** stitched together with brief narrator bridging lines.

The cold open serves three purposes:
1. **Hooks the viewer** with the most shocking moment of the case
2. **Introduces dramatic irony** -- the viewer glimpses the ending before the story begins
3. **Establishes the emotional stakes** -- death, betrayal, discovery

After the montage, the narrator delivers a **scene-setting paragraph** that rewinds to the beginning of the story, typically starting with a time/place anchor and victim introduction.

### 1.3 Cold Open Formula

The cold open follows a repeatable template:

1. **Real audio clip** (most dramatic moment -- often the discovery, the 911 call, or the arrest)
2. **Narrator identification line**: "This is [name], [age]-year-old [descriptor]." or "This is [name]. [One sentence describing their relationship to the case]."
3. **Dramatic irony setup**: "In just a few seconds, [he/she/they] [are] about to [dramatic event]." OR "[He/She] has no idea [shocking fact]."
4. **2-4 more audio clips** from different phases of the case (911, bodycam, interrogation), interleaved with narrator context
5. **Transition to story proper**: Time/place setting + victim/scene introduction

### 1.4 Key Cold Open Patterns

- **"This is..."** opening: 14 of 17 videos open with the narrator identifying a person using the formula "This is [name]" followed by a description.
- **Dramatic irony verb**: The cold open almost always uses future-tense constructions like "is about to," "is seconds away from," "will soon," creating a sense of impending doom.
- **Audio clip selection**: The montage typically pulls from at least 3 different phases of the case (911 call, bodycam discovery, interrogation confession).

## 2. Narrative Structure

### 2.1 Standard Act Structure (4 Acts)

Dr Insanity videos follow a consistent **4-act structure**:

**Act I: The Inciting Incident (~15-25% of runtime)**
- Scene-setting: time, place, victim lifestyle
- The triggering event (911 call, welfare check, discovery)
- Initial police response and first clues

**Act II: The Investigation (~35-45% of runtime)**
- Detectives dig deeper, interview witnesses
- Background on victim and suspect emerges
- Red herrings and conflicting stories surface
- Key evidence is found (financial records, phone data, CCTV)
- **Sponsor read typically falls at the boundary between Act II and Act III**

**Act III: The Confrontation (~20-30% of runtime)**
- Interrogation of the suspect(s)
- Lies unravel under pressure
- The confession or key evidence reveal
- The arrest

**Act IV: Resolution (~5-10% of runtime)**
- Trial outcome or current case status
- Sentencing details
- Final narrator commentary on the case
- Occasional call to action (GoFundMe, etc.)

### 2.2 Turning Point Placement

The major turning point -- where the case shifts from mystery to pursuit/confrontation -- typically occurs at **55-65%** of the video runtime. This is usually:
- The discovery of the body
- The confession from a witness
- The retrieval of CCTV footage
- A critical piece of forensic evidence arriving

### 2.3 Time Structure

The narrative is almost always **chronological** with two exceptions:
1. The cold open flash-forward
2. Occasional narrator-driven backstory insertions ("What they don't yet know is that [suspect] had a history of...")

## 3. Narrator Voice & Style

### 3.1 Tense

The narrator uses **present tense** for the primary narrative thread, creating a sense of unfolding events:
- "Officers are dispatched to the address."
- "Detective Curtis now has a body and what she believes is enough evidence."
- "As they arrive, they're unknowingly on the brink of walking into a murder scene."

**Past tense** is used only for:
- Background/backstory exposition ("She had already been married three times before")
- Describing events that happened before the main timeline ("Ryan's death was never as simple as it seemed")

### 3.2 Point of View

**Third person omniscient** -- the narrator knows everything but reveals it strategically:
- Tells us what police know and don't know
- Tells us what suspects are thinking/planning
- Withholds key information to create suspense, then reveals it at dramatic moments

The narrator frequently signals the gap between what characters know and what the viewer knows:
- "What [they] don't realize is..."
- "Officers have no idea that..."
- "Unknowingly, [they] are walking away from..."

### 3.3 Direct Address to Viewer

The narrator occasionally addresses the viewer directly, but sparingly:
- "As you can hear..." (during audio playback)
- Transitional empathy statements before sponsor reads: "And it's moments like these that you can realize just how fast your life can change."

The narrator **never** uses "we" to include the viewer. The viewer is always an observer, never a participant.

### 3.4 Sentence Length & Rhythm

Average sentence length: **15-22 words**. The narrator alternates between:
- Short declarative punches: "This is where things become very peculiar." (7 words)
- Medium narrative flow: "Officers are dispatched to the address to conduct a welfare check on the Thatford couple." (15 words)
- Longer compound sentences for context: "With all these findings combined, and Dena's shifting story, investigators begin to suspect they may be dealing with something far more serious than a missing person case, and the possibility of a concealed homicide now sits squarely on the table." (41 words)

The rhythm pattern is typically: **medium, medium, short punch, long context, short punch**. This creates a reading pace that builds momentum and then lands with impact.

### 3.5 Tone: Documentary Thriller

The tone balances:
- **Factual reporting** ("Following the 911 call, Officer Barnhill is dispatched to the address")
- **Dramatic tension** ("She has no idea of the amount of suspicion that's been mounting against her")
- **Emotional weight** ("Tomorrow was her birthday")
- **Restrained judgment** -- the narrator rarely explicitly condemns suspects; instead, the facts and audio speak for themselves

The narrator **never** sensationalizes with exclamation marks or hyperbolic adjectives. Drama comes from:
- Understatement ("Needless to say, this is not what investigators were hoping for")
- Factual revelation of horrific details
- Juxtaposition of calm narration against chaotic audio

## 4. How Real Audio/Video Is Integrated

### 4.1 Introducing 911 Calls

The narrator sets up the 911 call with a brief context line, then plays the audio:

**Standard formula:**
> "[Time/context], police receive [a/another/an alarming] [911 call / call / tip]."

Examples:
- "Following the 911 call, Officer Barnhill is dispatched to the address..."
- "At approximately 2:17 a.m. on that Sunday night, officers receive reports of gunshots..."
- "It's around 2:00 p.m. in the afternoon when Albuquerque police receive a routine welfare check call."
- "Then, around 1:30 a.m., a lone patrol unit spots a vehicle matching the description..."

After the 911 call plays, the narrator immediately contextualizes what was said:
- "On the line is [name] requesting/explaining..."
- "The caller also warns them that..."
- "[Name] would go on to explain that..."

### 4.2 Introducing Bodycam Footage

Bodycam is introduced by describing the officer's arrival and what they're about to encounter:

**Standard formula:**
> "[Officer name/rank] is [sent/dispatched] to [location] to [purpose]. [Setup for what they'll find]."

Examples:
- "Deputy Roger Schoolcraft is sent to check if Dena returned home and can provide any answers. In a few seconds, he will come face to face with the one person investigators now believe may be their prime suspect."
- "When he arrives, he finds Marilyn's younger brother waiting outside the home. Neither of them have any idea what disturbing clues are waiting inside the house."

### 4.3 Introducing Interrogation Clips

Interrogation footage is typically introduced by:
1. Describing the suspect's emotional state
2. Noting how much time has passed
3. Setting up what detectives hope to learn

**Standard formula:**
> "[Time reference] after [the shooting/arrest/incident], [suspect] is [brought in / placed in / transported to] [the interrogation room / the station]. [He/She] appears [emotional state]. [Context about what detectives need]."

Examples:
- "Approximately 6 hours after the shooting, investigators receive a critical update. Daniel has stabilized and is asking to tell his side of the story."
- "When detectives sit down with Alyssa, they think they're on the verge of capturing a potential double murderer."

### 4.4 Ratio of Narration to Real Audio

Approximate ratio: **60% narration / 40% real audio clips**. Real audio is never left to stand alone for more than ~60 seconds without narrator context. The narrator always:
- Sets up what we're about to hear
- Bridges between audio clips with context
- Summarizes or highlights key statements after audio plays

### 4.5 Handling Quotes from Witnesses

Witness statements are **always played as actual audio** when available, never read by the narrator. The narrator's role is strictly to introduce and contextualize. When actual audio is unavailable, the narrator paraphrases:
- "According to [name], [paraphrased statement]."
- "[Name] would go on to explain that..."

## 5. Tension & Retention Techniques

### 5.1 Open Loops

Open loops are the primary retention device. The narrator creates them by:

**Promising upcoming information:**
- "But that is about to change."
- "But [they/he/she] won't have to [wait/weigh those possibilities] for long because..."
- "What [they] don't yet realize is that..."
- "A detail that will soon become central to solving this case."
- "But this discovery would be just the start."
- "Details officers will soon uncover on their own."
- "A move that will not only fuel [X]'s growing suspicions, but also reveal..."

**Foreshadowing doom:**
- "And while [they] prepare to [action], [suspect] has already [dangerous action]."
- "But if [suspect] manages to [escape/flee/etc.], they may never [find the body/solve the case]."
- "What [he/she] doesn't know yet is that [devastating revelation]."
- "But by the time investigators catch on to the lies, both killers would walk free."

**Teasing future reveals before sponsor reads:**
- "But before we get to that..." (always precedes the sponsor)

### 5.2 Dramatic Irony

Dramatic irony is the most frequently used tension technique. The narrator explicitly tells the viewer something the characters don't know:

- "Unknowingly, officers are walking away from the first signs that something far more sinister is at play."
- "As Roger checks the safe, he has no idea that he is standing in the very spot where Craig's body remained for days."
- "Isaac was far more involved in the murder than he's letting on and he knows it's only a matter of time before investigators start to catch on."
- "Police don't know it yet, but he's showing them the exact building where a missing teenager was murdered just days earlier."
- "Officers had no way of knowing that the biggest clue they were looking for was submerged 20 ft deep in a pond nearby."

### 5.3 Cliffhanger Placement

Cliffhangers are placed at:
1. **Right before the sponsor read** (the most consistent cliffhanger position)
2. **At act transitions** -- just as a new phase of the investigation begins
3. **Before revealing the contents of key evidence** (CCTV footage, phone messages, safe contents)

### 5.4 Pacing: New Information Arrival Rate

New information arrives approximately every **60-90 seconds**. This can be:
- A new audio clip
- A new witness statement
- A detective's discovery
- A narrator revelation of something the viewer didn't know

The pacing accelerates toward the climax (confession/arrest) and decelerates during the resolution.

### 5.5 Pattern Interrupts

- **Tonal shifts**: Moving from calm scene-setting to a sudden 911 call or gunshot
- **Perspective shifts**: Switching from the investigation to the suspect's POV
- **Time jumps**: "Five days later..." or "In the following weeks..."
- **Rhetorical questions** (rare): "Why was Craig truly killed?"

## 6. Character Introduction

### 6.1 Victims

Victims are introduced with **warmth and specificity**, establishing them as real people:

**Formula:** "[Age]-year-old [Name] [was/is] [occupation/lifestyle detail]. [He/She] lives [location] with [family]. [1-2 sentences about their life/personality]."

Examples:
- "22-year-old Katie Blauvelt is captured on CCTV inside her workplace. Her shift is soon coming to an end."
- "65-year-old Marilyn Gandert, was last seen walking outside her home before disappearing without a trace."
- "He lives inside this large three-story house with his wife, Dena. The Thatfords enjoy a comfortable lifestyle thanks to Craig's roofing business."
- "She's a postal worker who adores her dogs and would never leave them alone."
- "At just 19, he purchased 640 acres of land with his grandfather for an estimated 200 to $300,000 and built a reputation for his ambitious dreams of running a huge farm."

### 6.2 Suspects

Suspects are introduced with **behavioral red flags** and **ominous foreshadowing**:

**Formula:** "[Name/relationship to victim]. [Something seemingly normal]. But [ominous detail]."

Examples:
- "And this is Daniel Krug, the man who killed her. He's heading in for another normal workday just minutes after leaving his wife's lifeless body hidden in the garage."
- "Craig is Dena's fourth husband. She had already been married three times before and those relationships seemingly ended under troubling circumstances."
- "This is Jim Brener, a farmer living in the remote Utah desert. Police don't know it yet, but he's showing them the exact building where a missing teenager was murdered."

### 6.3 Investigators/Police

Officers and detectives are introduced with **professional context** and occasionally **personal stakes**:

- "Detective Bobby Curtis with the district's major crimes unit"
- "Lead Detective Frasier of the Mcome County Sheriff's Office. Frasier quickly begins identifying the people he and his team will need to focus on."
- "The officer assigned as the sniper is Jared Tadahara, a long-serving police officer who moved from patrol and street crime work into a specialized SWAT sniper role."
- "Detective Vigil... Javier is an experienced detective who as of 2025 has been promoted to SWAT commander for SFPD."
- "Lead detective immediately recognizes the name -- 28-year-old Ashley Fish. He knew Ashley as a Ditter local, someone he had known for years."

### 6.4 Witnesses

Witnesses are introduced with **their relationship to the case** and **what they know**:

- "On the line is Larry Scott requesting a welfare check on his friend."
- "This is Harry Gandert, Marilyn's brother. He sounds genuinely worried for his sister, especially since she's the only family he has left."
- "Briana's cousin would go on to explain that the family hasn't seen Brianna's daughter in nearly a year."

## 8. Sponsor Integration

### 8.1 Placement

The sponsor read is placed at approximately **35-50% through the video**, always at a moment of maximum suspense -- typically right after a major cliffhanger or right before a major reveal.

### 8.2 Transition INTO the Sponsor

The narrator uses one of two formulas:

**Formula A (Suspense bridge):**
> "But before we get to that, [relatable life observation that connects thematically to the sponsor]."

**Formula B (Empathy bridge):**
> "And it's moments like these that you can realize just how fast your life can change. [Sponsor pitch]."

Examples:
- "But before we get to that, if you're like me, you probably want to save money and have your money actually work for you without over complicating things or constantly thinking about it. That's where our video sponsor, Chime, comes in."
- "But before we get to that, most days are long, stressful, and by the time dinner comes around, the last thing I want to do is figure out what to make."
- "But before we get to that, these cases often affect the mental health of both the officers involved and the innocent people involved..."

### 8.3 Transition OUT of the Sponsor

Always the same formula:
> "With that said, let's get back to [the investigation / the scene / Detective X / the interrogation] where [setup for next scene]."

Examples:
- "With that said, let's get back to Deputy Roger, who is on the verge of hearing a story that may explain why Craig truly vanished."
- "With that said, let's get back to the investigation where Daniel has just been brought in from the hospital."
- "With that said, let's get back to the investigation where detectives are about to speak with Marilyn's grandson."

### 8.5 Sponsor Read Style

Sponsor reads are delivered in **first person** ("I use it," "I've been using"), are 60-90 seconds long, and always include:
1. A personal anecdote connecting the narrator to the product
2. 2-3 key product benefits
3. A specific URL (typically forthepeople.com/drinsanity, betterhelp.com/drinsanity, etc.)
4. A mention of the "link in the description" or QR code

## 9. Closing Formula

### 9.1 Standard Closing Structure

The closing follows a consistent 3-part formula:

**Part 1: Legal Outcome**
> "[Suspect] is [charged with / found guilty of / sentenced to] [specific charges]. [He/She] [faces / received] [sentence length] [with/without parole]."

Examples:
- "Dena is booked into jail without bond for first-degree murder and tampering with evidence."
- "Matthew is formally charged with attempted murder... voluntary manslaughter... and multiple counts of felony firearm."
- "Tyler would be found guilty on all charges and sentenced to life in prison with no chance of parole."
- "He is sentenced to a maximum of 40 years with parole possible after roughly 18 years."

**Part 2: Case Context / Investigator Summary**
A brief explanation of the investigative conclusion or what the evidence ultimately proved:
- "Investigators concluded that Joseph's actions were driven by a rapid collapse."
- "Investigators later determined that Devon Wayland acted alone, ruling out connections to any larger extremist groups."
- "Prosecutors believe what they have will lead to a conviction with the main open question being whether the jury finds first-degree murder or a lesser homicide charge."

**Part 3: Current Status (if applicable)**
For ongoing cases:
- "As of [month year], [name]'s case is still in the pre-trial phase and [he/she] remains innocent until proven guilty in court."
- "As of September 2025, both have been released on bond while awaiting trial."

### 9.2 Emotional Closing (Occasional)

Some videos end with a more emotional or reflective note:
- The GoFundMe announcement for Ashley Fish's children: "We've created a GoFundMe for these children, starting with a $10,000 donation of our own."
- Reflections on prevention: "It sheds light on the importance of closely monitoring your children as you never know what's going on in their lives."
- Emphasis on accountability: "Thankfully, the three victims wounded in the shooting, including Officer Cody Neim, would ultimately recover from their physical injuries."

### 9.3 What is NOT in the Closing

- No "like and subscribe" call to action (this is notably absent)
- No preview of the next video
- No personal narrator sign-off catchphrase
- No music credits or team acknowledgments (in the transcript text)

## 10. Writing a Dr Insanity Screenplay: Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Map the Real Audio Assets

Before writing, catalog all available audio/video:
- 911 calls (with timestamps)
- Bodycam footage (with key moments)
- Interrogation recordings (with key admissions)
- CCTV footage
- Witness interviews

### Step 2: Build the Cold Open Montage

Select 3-7 of the most dramatic audio clips. Arrange them to create a "trailer" effect:
1. Start with the most visually/aurally striking clip (discovery, gunshots, screaming)
2. Add narrator identification: "This is [name]. [Dramatic irony setup]."
3. Weave in 2-4 more clips with brief narrator bridges
4. End the montage with a cliffhanger or dramatic reveal

### Step 3: Write Act I (The Setup)

- Rewind to the beginning: "It's [time] on [day] in [location]."
- Introduce the victim with warmth and specificity
- Play the triggering event (911 call, welfare check request)
- Show officers arriving and their initial observations
- End Act I with the first major clue or escalation

### Step 4: Write Act II (The Investigation)

- Deepen the mystery through interviews and background research
- Build open loops: "What they don't realize is..."
- Introduce the suspect's history and red flags
- Show conflicting stories from different witnesses
- Build toward the sponsor read cliffhanger: "But [critical development] is about to happen. But before we get to that..."

### Step 5: Insert Sponsor Read

- Bridge thematically from the case to the sponsor
- Deliver 60-90 second read
- Bridge back: "With that said, let's get back to..."

### Step 6: Write Act III (The Confrontation)

- Play the key interrogation footage
- Use dramatic irony: "What [suspect] doesn't know is that police already have [evidence]."
- Show the moment the suspect's story falls apart
- Play the confession or key evidence reveal
- Show the arrest

### Step 7: Write Act IV (Resolution)

- State the charges and legal outcome
- Provide case context (investigator conclusions)
- Note current case status if applicable
- Optional: emotional note, GoFundMe, or systemic observation

### Key Rules to Follow

1. **Always use present tense** for the main narrative thread
2. **Never editorialize** -- let the facts and audio speak
3. **Every audio clip must be set up** by the narrator before playing
4. **New information every 60-90 seconds** -- never let the narrative go flat
5. **Maintain at least 2 open loops** at all times throughout the middle acts
6. **Use dramatic irony liberally** -- the viewer should always know more than the characters
7. **Alternate sentence lengths** -- short punches followed by flowing context
8. **The sponsor read is a cliffhanger** -- always place it at maximum suspense
