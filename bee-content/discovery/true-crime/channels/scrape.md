3. Direct Agency Transparency Pages
Many large law enforcement agencies now skip the media and post footage directly to their own dedicated portals to meet transparency requirements.

LAPD Online Newsroom: The Los Angeles Police Department is one of the most active in releasing "Critical Incident Videos" which combine 911 audio, bodycam, and narrated context.

Phoenix Police Transparency: Similar to the LAPD, they maintain a very active YouTube playlist and web portal for every officer-involved shooting or major use-of-force event.

Las Vegas Metro Police (LVMPD): They hold regular press conferences for major cases and upload the full video clips used in those briefings to their website and YouTube channel.

4. Community Aggregators
If you want to see footage as it is leaked or released before it hits major YouTube channels, these communities are highly active:

Reddit (r/Bodycam & r/PoliceActivity): These subreddits act as hubs where users post links to new footage from small-town departments that might not get national news coverage.

X (formerly Twitter): Accounts like @Police_Activity or local "Breaking News" handles for specific cities often post snippets of 911 audio or bystander video minutes after an incident occurs.

5. News "Raw" Sections
Local news stations (e.g., KTVU, ABC7, or NBC affiliates) often have "Raw Video" or "Full Interview" sections on their websites. When a department releases a 30-minute interrogation but the news only shows 10 seconds, they will often host the full, unedited file on their website for the public to view.
