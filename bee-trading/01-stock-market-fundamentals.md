## 1. Stock Market Fundamentals

### 1.1 What Is the Stock Market?

The stock market is a marketplace where buyers and sellers trade shares (small ownership units) of publicly listed companies. When you buy a stock, you are buying a tiny piece of ownership in that company.

**How it works at a high level:**

1. A company decides it wants to raise money. It "goes public" through an **IPO (Initial Public Offering)**, selling shares to the public for the first time.
2. Those shares then trade on an **exchange** (like the NYSE or NASDAQ), where anyone with a brokerage account can buy or sell them.
3. The price of a stock moves up and down based on **supply and demand** -- if more people want to buy a stock than sell it, the price goes up, and vice versa.
4. Investors make money in two ways: **capital gains** (buying low and selling high) and **dividends** (a portion of the company's profits paid out to shareholders).

**Why do stock prices move?**

- Company earnings reports (quarterly financial results)
- Economic data (jobs reports, inflation, interest rates)
- News and events (product launches, lawsuits, geopolitical events)
- Investor sentiment and speculation
- Supply and demand dynamics

#### User-Specific Market Access Notes

> **⚠️ User 1 (H1B/US):** You can trade freely on US exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ, AMEX) as described above. H1B visa holders are allowed to earn passive investment income (capital gains, dividends, interest) without any visa restrictions. Day trading and swing trading are permitted as passive investing activities. **However**, if trading becomes your primary occupation or you run it as a full-time trading business (e.g., forming an LLC to trade, treating it as self-employment), it could be viewed as unauthorized employment outside your H1B sponsor -- this is a legal gray area. Stick to treating it as personal investing/side activity, not a business. You cannot earn active business income outside your H1B employer.

> **⚠️ User 2 (India):** You primarily trade on Indian exchanges -- **NSE (National Stock Exchange)** and **BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange)** -- via Indian brokers such as **Zerodha, Groww, Upstox, or Angel One**. To access US markets, you can use the **LRS (Liberalized Remittance Scheme)**, which allows Indian residents to remit up to **$250,000 per financial year** for overseas investments. Platforms for US investing include **Vested, INDmoney, Stockal** (Indian platforms that facilitate US stock purchases), or **Interactive Brokers** (international account). You must comply with **RBI (Reserve Bank of India)** and **SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India)** regulations at all times. All foreign investments must be reported in your income tax return.

### 1.2 Key Terminology

#### Stocks (Equities)

A **stock** represents partial ownership in a company. Owning 1 share of Apple means you own a tiny fraction of Apple Inc. Stocks are the most common instrument beginners trade.

- **Common stock**: Gives you voting rights at shareholder meetings and potential dividends. This is what most people mean when they say "stock."
- **Preferred stock**: Pays a fixed dividend and has priority over common stock if the company goes bankrupt, but usually no voting rights.
- **Penny stocks**: Stocks priced below $5 per share, often from very small companies. Extremely risky and volatile -- generally not recommended for beginners.
- **Blue-chip stocks**: Shares of large, well-established, financially stable companies (e.g., Apple, Microsoft, Johnson & Johnson). Considered safer investments.

#### Bonds

A **bond** is essentially a loan you give to a company or government. In return, they pay you interest over a set period and return your principal at maturity.

- Lower risk than stocks, but lower potential returns.
- Government bonds (Treasury bonds) are considered among the safest investments.
- Corporate bonds pay higher interest but carry more risk.
- Bond prices move inversely to interest rates -- when rates go up, bond prices go down.

#### ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)

An **ETF** is a basket of securities (stocks, bonds, commodities) that trades on an exchange like a single stock. Think of it as a pre-made diversified portfolio.

- **Example**: The SPY ETF tracks the S&P 500, so buying one share gives you exposure to 500 of the largest U.S. companies.
- Lower fees than most mutual funds.
- Great for beginners because they provide instant diversification.
- Types: Index ETFs, sector ETFs (technology, healthcare), bond ETFs, commodity ETFs, international ETFs.

#### Mutual Funds

Similar to ETFs but managed by a professional fund manager who actively picks investments. They are priced once per day (after market close), while ETFs trade throughout the day. Generally have higher fees than ETFs.

#### Options

An **option** is a contract that gives you the right (but not the obligation) to buy or sell a stock at a specific price by a specific date.

- **Call option**: The right to BUY a stock at a set price (you profit if the stock goes UP).
- **Put option**: The right to SELL a stock at a set price (you profit if the stock goes DOWN).
- **Strike price**: The price at which you can buy/sell the stock.
- **Expiration date**: The deadline by which you must exercise the option.
- **Premium**: The price you pay to buy the option contract.

Options are **advanced instruments**. They can amplify gains but also amplify losses. Each contract typically represents 100 shares. Beginners should learn stocks thoroughly before touching options.

#### Futures

A **futures contract** is an agreement to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a specific future date. Used heavily in commodities (oil, gold, wheat) and indices.

- Both parties are **obligated** to fulfill the contract (unlike options).
- Highly leveraged -- small price movements create large gains or losses.
- Not recommended for beginners.

#### Forex (Foreign Exchange)

**Forex** is the market for trading currencies (e.g., USD/EUR, USD/JPY). It is the largest financial market in the world with over $7 trillion in daily volume.

- Trades in currency pairs -- you are always buying one currency and selling another.
- Open 24 hours a day, 5 days a week.
- Heavily leveraged (100:1 or more is common), making it very risky.
- Not recommended for beginners despite its accessibility.

#### Crypto (Cryptocurrency)

**Cryptocurrency** is a digital asset that uses blockchain technology. Bitcoin (BTC) and Ethereum (ETH) are the most well-known.

- Extremely volatile -- 10-20% price swings in a single day are not unusual.
- Trades 24/7/365 (never closes).
- Not regulated in the same way as stocks.
- Exchanges include Coinbase, Kraken, and Binance.
- Beginners should be very cautious and only invest what they can afford to lose entirely.

| Term | Definition |

### 1.3 Types of Trading

#### Day Trading

- **Timeframe**: Positions opened and closed within the same trading day. Nothing held overnight.
- **Goal**: Profit from small intraday price movements.
- **Frequency**: Multiple trades per day (sometimes dozens or hundreds).
- **Requires**: Fast decision-making, strong technical analysis skills, significant capital, and substantial screen time.
- **Important rule**: The **Pattern Day Trader (PDT) rule** requires a minimum of $25,000 in your account if you make 4 or more day trades within 5 business days on a margin account. This is a FINRA regulation.
- **Success rate**: Studies estimate only 10-15% of day traders are consistently profitable. Most beginners lose money.

#### Swing Trading

- **Timeframe**: Positions held for days to weeks (sometimes a few months).
- **Goal**: Capture "swings" or trends in stock prices.
- **Frequency**: A few trades per week.
- **Requires**: Combination of technical and fundamental analysis. Less time-intensive than day trading.
- **Best for**: People who have jobs and cannot watch markets all day. Often considered the best starting style for beginners who want to actively trade.

#### Position Trading (Long-Term Investing)

- **Timeframe**: Positions held for months to years.
- **Goal**: Benefit from long-term growth and compounding.
- **Frequency**: A few trades per month or less.
- **Requires**: Strong fundamental analysis, patience, and conviction.
- **Best for**: Building wealth over time. This is what Warren Buffett does. Historically, the S&P 500 has returned approximately 10% per year on average over the long term.

#### Scalping

- **Timeframe**: Positions held for seconds to minutes.
- **Goal**: Profit from tiny price movements, often just a few cents per share.
- **Frequency**: Dozens to hundreds of trades per day.
- **Requires**: Very fast execution, low-latency systems, tight spreads, and extreme discipline.
- **Not recommended for beginners at all.** This is for experienced traders with professional-grade tools.

#### Comparison Table

| Scalping | Seconds-minutes | 50-200+ | Full-time | No |
| Day Trading | Minutes-hours | 5-50 | Full-time | No |

### 1.5 Order Types

Understanding order types is critical. Placing the wrong order type can cost you money.

#### Market Order

- **What it does**: Buys or sells immediately at the best available current price.
- **When to use**: When you want to get in or out of a position RIGHT NOW and speed matters more than price.
- **Pros**: Guaranteed execution (as long as the market is open and the stock has liquidity).
- **Cons**: You might get a slightly different price than you expected, especially with volatile or low-volume stocks. This difference is called **slippage**.

#### Limit Order

- **What it does**: Buys or sells only at your specified price or better.
  - **Buy limit**: Executes at your price or LOWER.
  - **Sell limit**: Executes at your price or HIGHER.
- **When to use**: When you want to control the exact price you pay or receive.
- **Pros**: No slippage -- you get your price or better.
- **Cons**: Your order may never fill if the price doesn't reach your limit. You might miss the trade entirely.

#### Stop-Loss Order (Stop Order)

- **What it does**: Becomes a market order once the stock hits a specified price (the "stop price"). Used to limit losses.
- **Example**: You buy a stock at $50 and set a stop-loss at $45. If the stock drops to $45, your stop-loss triggers and sells your shares at the next available price.
- **When to use**: To protect yourself from large losses. Essential for risk management.
- **Cons**: In a fast-moving market, the actual sell price might be lower than your stop price (slippage). "Flash crashes" can trigger your stop-loss on a temporary dip.

#### Stop-Limit Order

- **What it does**: Combines a stop order and a limit order. Once the stop price is hit, it places a limit order (not a market order).
- **Example**: Stop price at $45, limit at $44.50. If the stock drops to $45, a limit order is placed to sell at $44.50 or better.
- **Pros**: Gives you more control over execution price.
- **Cons**: In a fast crash, your limit might not fill at all, leaving you holding a losing position.

#### Trailing Stop Order

- **What it does**: A stop-loss that moves with the stock price. You set a trailing amount (dollar or percentage).
- **Example**: You buy at $50 with a 10% trailing stop. If the stock rises to $60, your stop moves to $54 (10% below $60). If it then drops to $54, it triggers a sell.
- **When to use**: To lock in profits while still allowing the stock to run higher.

#### Other Order Modifiers

| Modifier | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| **GTC (Good Till Cancelled)** | Order stays active until filled or you cancel it (up to a broker-defined time limit, often 60-90 days) |
| **Day order** | Order expires at the end of the trading day if not filled |
| **AON (All or None)** | Order only fills if the entire quantity can be executed at once |
| **IOC (Immediate or Cancel)** | Fill as much as possible immediately, cancel the rest |
| **FOK (Fill or Kill)** | Fill the entire order immediately or cancel it entirely |

## 2. Getting Started

### 2.1 How to Open a Brokerage Account

A **brokerage account** is like a bank account, but for investing. You deposit money, and then use it to buy and sell stocks, ETFs, and other securities.

**Steps to open an account:**

1. **Choose a brokerage** (see recommendations below).
2. **Apply online** -- you'll need:
   - Full legal name and date of birth
   - Social Security Number (SSN) or Tax ID
   - Home address
   - Employment information
   - Bank account for funding (routing and account numbers)
3. **Choose account type**:
   - **Individual taxable account**: Standard brokerage account. No tax advantages, but no restrictions on withdrawals.
   - **Roth IRA**: Retirement account. Contributions are after-tax, but growth and withdrawals are tax-free after age 59.5. Contribution limit is $7,000/year (2025-2026) for those under 50.
   - **Traditional IRA**: Retirement account. Contributions may be tax-deductible, but withdrawals are taxed.
   - **Margin account**: Lets you borrow money from the broker to trade. Risky for beginners -- you can lose more than you deposit.
   - **Cash account**: You can only trade with the money you have deposited. Safer for beginners. No PDT rule restrictions.
4. **Fund your account** via bank transfer (ACH), wire transfer, or check.
5. **Start trading** once funds settle (typically 1-3 business days for ACH).

### 2.3 Paper Trading / Simulated Trading

**Paper trading** means trading with fake (virtual) money in a simulated market environment. The prices are real, but no actual money is at risk.

**Why paper trade:**

- Learn how the market works without risking real money.
- Test trading strategies.
- Get comfortable with your brokerage platform.
- Build confidence before going live.
- Develop discipline and emotional control.

**Where to paper trade:**

| Platform | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **thinkorswim (Schwab)** | The gold standard for paper trading. Full-featured simulation. |
| **Webull** | Built-in paper trading with $1,000,000 virtual balance. |
| **TradingView** | Excellent charting platform with paper trading feature. Free tier available. |
| **Investopedia Stock Simulator** | Web-based, great for complete beginners. |

**How long to paper trade:**

- At minimum, 1-3 months before using real money.
- Paper trade until you are consistently profitable (or at least not losing significantly) over at least 50-100 trades.
- Keep a trading journal during paper trading, just as you would with real money.

**Limitations of paper trading:**

- No emotional pressure -- real money feels very different from fake money.
- Execution may be slightly unrealistic (fills happen perfectly in simulation, but not always in real markets).
- It can create overconfidence.

### 2.4 Minimum Capital Requirements

There is no legal minimum to open most brokerage accounts, but practical minimums depend on your goals:

| Situation | Suggested Minimum | Notes |
|-----------|------------------|-------|
| **Long-term investing (buy and hold)** | $100-$500 | Many brokers offer fractional shares. Even $50/month invested consistently can grow significantly over decades. |
| **Swing trading** | $2,000-$5,000 | Need enough capital to diversify across a few positions and absorb losses without being wiped out. |
| **Day trading (margin account)** | $25,000+ | PDT rule requires $25,000 minimum equity for pattern day traders. Below this, you are limited to 3 day trades per 5 business days. |
| **Day trading (cash account)** | $5,000-$10,000 | Cash accounts avoid PDT rules, but you must wait for trades to settle (T+1 for stocks as of 2024). |
| **Options trading** | $2,000-$5,000 | Depends on strategies used. Some brokers require minimum balances for options approval. |

**Key principle**: Only invest money you can afford to lose. Never trade with rent money, emergency funds, or borrowed money (especially as a beginner).

### 2.5 Tax Implications Basics

Trading profits are taxable. Understanding the basics now will save you headaches later.

#### Capital Gains Tax

- **Short-term capital gains**: Profits from assets held less than 1 year. Taxed as ordinary income (your regular tax bracket, which can be 10-37% federally).
- **Long-term capital gains**: Profits from assets held more than 1 year. Taxed at preferential rates: 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your income.
- **This is a major reason long-term investing is tax-advantaged over frequent trading.**

#### Capital Losses

- Losses can offset gains. If you made $5,000 in gains and $3,000 in losses, you only pay tax on $2,000 net gain.
- If your losses exceed your gains, you can deduct up to $3,000 per year from ordinary income.
- Excess losses carry forward to future years.

#### Wash Sale Rule

- If you sell a security at a loss and buy the same or a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale, the loss is **disallowed** for tax purposes.
- This means you cannot sell a stock for a tax loss and immediately buy it back.
- The disallowed loss gets added to the cost basis of the new shares.

#### Tax Documents

- Your broker will send you a **1099-B** form showing all your trades for the year.
- You report these on **Schedule D** and **Form 8949** of your tax return.
- Most tax software (TurboTax, H&R Block) can import 1099-B data directly from your broker.

#### Tax-Advantaged Accounts

- **IRA and 401(k) accounts**: No taxes on gains within the account. Taxes are deferred (Traditional) or eliminated (Roth).
- Consider maxing out tax-advantaged accounts before trading in a taxable brokerage account.

#### Record Keeping

- Keep a log of all trades: date, ticker, quantity, price, fees.
- Your broker tracks this, but having your own records is good practice.
- Save records for at least 3 years (IRS audit window), ideally 7 years.

#### User-Specific Tax Obligations

##### **⚠️ User 1 (H1B/US) Tax Obligations**

As an H1B visa holder living and working in the US, you are treated as a **US resident alien for tax purposes** (assuming you pass the substantial presence test, which most H1B holders do). This means you are taxed on your **worldwide income** by the IRS.

**Capital Gains Tax (US):**
- **Short-term capital gains** (assets held < 1 year): Taxed as **ordinary income** at your marginal tax rate (10%-37% federal, depending on total income including W-2 salary).
- **Long-term capital gains** (assets held > 1 year): Taxed at preferential rates of **0%, 15%, or 20%** depending on your taxable income.
- **Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT):** An additional 3.8% surtax on investment income if your modified AGI exceeds $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly).

**Key Rules:**
- **Wash sale rule** applies to all US trades (see above).
- **State taxes** also apply depending on which state you live in. States like California and New York have high income taxes; states like Texas, Florida, and Washington have no state income tax.
- W-2 income + all trading income goes on your US federal tax return (Form 1040).

**Foreign Account Reporting (Critical for H1B holders with Indian accounts):**
- **FBAR (FinCEN Form 114):** You **MUST** file an FBAR if the aggregate balance of all your foreign (Indian) financial accounts exceeds **$10,000 at any point** during the calendar year. This includes bank accounts, NRE/NRO accounts, PPF, demat accounts, mutual fund folios, etc. Penalties for non-filing are severe (up to $10,000 per violation for non-willful; criminal penalties for willful violations).
- **FATCA (Form 8938):** You **MUST** file Form 8938 (Statement of Specified Foreign Financial Assets) if your foreign assets exceed the thresholds: **$50,000** on the last day of the year or **$75,000** at any time (single filers living in the US). Higher thresholds apply for married filing jointly ($100K/$150K). This is filed with your tax return, separate from FBAR.

**DTAA (Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement) -- US-India:**
- The US and India have a DTAA to prevent double taxation on the same income.
- If you earn income in India (e.g., rental income, interest on NRO accounts, capital gains on Indian investments), you may be taxed in India. You can claim a **Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116)** on your US return for taxes paid to India, avoiding double taxation.
- If you are an NRI for Indian tax purposes, only your Indian-source income is taxable in India. Your US salary and US trading income are not taxed in India.

**Employer Stock Options:**
- **ISOs (Incentive Stock Options):** No regular income tax at exercise (but may trigger AMT). Taxed at capital gains rates when shares are sold. Special holding period requirements.
- **NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options):** Taxed as ordinary income at exercise (difference between exercise price and FMV). Appears on W-2.
- **RSUs (Restricted Stock Units):** Taxed as ordinary income when they vest. Appears on W-2.
- Consult a CPA or tax advisor familiar with H1B/immigrant tax situations for stock compensation.

**Recommended Tools:**
- Tax software: **TurboTax** (Premier or Self-Employed), **H&R Block**, or **FreeTaxUSA** (for simpler returns).
- For complex situations (FBAR, FATCA, DTAA credits, stock options): Hire a **CPA familiar with H1B/NRI tax situations**. Look for CPAs who specialize in Indian expat taxes.

##### **⚠️ User 2 (India) Tax Obligations**

As an Indian resident, all your worldwide income is taxable in India. Even though you are currently unemployed, if your total income (including trading income) exceeds the **basic exemption limit**, you must file an ITR.

**Stocks -- Indian Market (NSE/BSE):**
- **STCG (Short-Term Capital Gains):** Stocks/equity mutual funds held < 1 year. Taxed at a flat rate of **15%** (plus cess and surcharge). This applies only to listed equities where STT has been paid.
- **LTCG (Long-Term Capital Gains):** Stocks/equity mutual funds held > 1 year. Taxed at **10% on gains above ₹1 lakh per financial year** (no indexation benefit). Gains up to ₹1 lakh are tax-free.
- **STT (Securities Transaction Tax):** Automatically deducted at the time of transaction by the exchange. This is separate from income tax.

**Stocks -- US Market (via LRS):**
- Gains from foreign stocks are taxed as per your **income tax slab rates** (there is no special STCG/LTCG distinction like domestic equities -- check the latest Finance Act as rules have been evolving post-2024).
- You **must report all foreign assets** in your ITR under **Schedule FA (Foreign Assets)** and **Schedule FSI (Foreign Source Income)**.
- **TCS (Tax Collected at Source):** Banks collect **20% TCS on LRS remittances above ₹7 lakh per financial year**. This is not an additional tax -- you can claim it as a **credit while filing your ITR** and get it refunded if your total tax liability is lower.

**Cryptocurrency:**
- **30% flat tax** on all crypto gains (Section 115BBH). No deductions are allowed except the cost of acquisition (not even transaction fees or internet costs).
- **1% TDS** on all crypto transactions above ₹10,000/year (deducted by the exchange).
- **Losses from crypto cannot be set off** against any other income (including other crypto gains in some interpretations -- consult a CA).
- **Crypto losses cannot be carried forward** to future years.
- This is one of the harshest crypto tax regimes globally. Factor this into your expected returns.

**Futures & Options (F&O):**
- F&O income is treated as **business income** (not capital gains), regardless of how frequently you trade.
- Taxed at your **income tax slab rates**.
- **Tax audit required** if your F&O turnover exceeds **₹10 crore** (for digital transactions; ₹2 crore for non-digital).
- **Turnover calculation for F&O:** Absolute sum of profit and loss (not the notional contract value).
- You **can claim trading-related expenses** as deductions: internet charges, brokerage, advisory fees, depreciation on computer/equipment, etc.
- If you show a loss in F&O, you must get a tax audit done to carry forward the loss.

**Important -- Unemployed Does Not Mean Tax-Free:**
- Even without employment income, if your total income from trading, investments, interest, or any other source exceeds the **basic exemption limit (₹2.5 lakh for individuals under 60; ₹3 lakh under the new tax regime)**, you **must file an ITR**.
- Filing ITR is also required if you hold foreign assets (regardless of income level), if you want to carry forward losses, or if TDS/TCS has been deducted and you want a refund.

**Recommended Tools:**
- **Cleartax** or **Quicko** for easy ITR filing (both support capital gains, F&O, and foreign income reporting).
- Hire a **Chartered Accountant (CA)** if you trade F&O, hold foreign assets, or have complex tax situations.

## 3. Key Concepts

### 3.1 Technical Analysis vs. Fundamental Analysis

These are the two main schools of thought for evaluating stocks.

#### Fundamental Analysis

**What**: Evaluating a company's intrinsic value based on its financial health, earnings, revenue, assets, industry position, and economic conditions.

**Key question**: "Is this company a good business worth owning at this price?"

**What you look at:**

- **Income statement**: Revenue, net income, profit margins.
- **Balance sheet**: Assets, liabilities, debt levels, cash on hand.
- **Cash flow statement**: How much cash the business actually generates.
- **Valuation ratios**:
  - **P/E ratio** (Price/Earnings): How much you pay per dollar of earnings. Lower = potentially cheaper.
  - **P/B ratio** (Price/Book): Stock price vs. book value of assets.
  - **P/S ratio** (Price/Sales): Stock price vs. revenue.
  - **PEG ratio** (P/E to Growth): P/E adjusted for earnings growth rate. < 1 may indicate undervaluation.
- **Industry and competitive position**: Market share, competitive advantages ("moats").
- **Management quality**: Track record, insider ownership, capital allocation decisions.
- **Macroeconomic factors**: Interest rates, inflation, GDP growth, employment data.

**Best for**: Long-term investing, position trading, value investing.

**Key figures**: Warren Buffett, Benjamin Graham, Peter Lynch.

#### Technical Analysis

**What**: Studying price charts, patterns, and statistical indicators to predict future price movements, regardless of the underlying company.

**Key question**: "Where is the price likely to go next based on historical patterns?"

**Core principles:**

1. **Price discounts everything**: All known information (fundamentals, news, sentiment) is already reflected in the stock price.
2. **Price moves in trends**: Prices tend to continue moving in a direction until something causes a reversal.
3. **History tends to repeat**: Patterns that worked in the past tend to work again because human psychology is consistent.

**What you look at:**

- **Price charts**: Candlestick charts, line charts, bar charts.
- **Support and resistance levels**: Prices where a stock historically bounces (support) or gets rejected (resistance).
- **Trend lines**: Lines drawn on charts connecting highs or lows to identify the direction of price movement.
- **Chart patterns**: Head and shoulders, double tops/bottoms, triangles, flags, wedges.
- **Indicators**: RSI, MACD, moving averages, Bollinger Bands, volume (covered in detail below).
- **Candlestick patterns**: Doji, hammer, engulfing patterns, morning/evening star.

**Best for**: Day trading, swing trading, timing entries and exits.

#### Which Should You Use?

**Both.** Most successful traders use a combination:

- Use **fundamental analysis** to decide WHAT to buy (find good companies).
- Use **technical analysis** to decide WHEN to buy (find good entry and exit points).

### 3.2 Risk Management Basics

Risk management is arguably the MOST important skill in trading. It is more important than picking winning stocks.

#### The Golden Rules

1. **Never risk more than 1-2% of your total account on a single trade.**
   - If your account is $10,000, never risk more than $100-$200 on any single trade.
   - This means if your stop-loss is $2 below your entry price, you should buy no more than 50-100 shares.
   - This protects you from catastrophic losses. Even 10 losing trades in a row only costs 10-20% of your account.

2. **Always use stop-losses.**
   - Decide your exit point BEFORE entering the trade.
   - Never move your stop-loss further away from your entry. That is a sign of hope overriding discipline.

3. **Maintain a favorable risk-to-reward ratio.**
   - Aim for at least 1:2 risk-to-reward. If you risk $1, your profit target should be at least $2.
   - This means you can be wrong 50% of the time and still be profitable.
   - Many professionals aim for 1:3 or better.

4. **Position sizing matters.**
   - Don't put all your money into one stock.
   - Calculate your position size based on your stop-loss distance and the amount you are willing to risk.
   - Formula: Position Size = (Account Risk Amount) / (Entry Price - Stop-Loss Price)

5. **Preserve capital above all else.**
   - Your first priority is not to make money; it is to not lose money.
   - If you preserve your capital, you will always have the opportunity to trade another day.

#### Risk Management Tools

- **Stop-loss orders**: Automatically sell if the price drops to a certain level.
- **Position sizing calculators**: Tools that help determine how many shares to buy.
- **Diversification**: Spread risk across multiple stocks and sectors.
- **Correlation awareness**: If all your stocks are in the same sector, they will likely all drop together. That is not true diversification.

#### User-Specific Risk Management Notes

> **⚠️ User 1 (H1B/US):** The **PDT (Pattern Day Trader) rule** is a critical constraint. If you make **4 or more day trades within 5 business days** in a **margin account**, you are classified as a pattern day trader and must maintain at least **$25,000 in equity** at all times. If your balance falls below $25,000, your account will be restricted. **How to avoid PDT issues:** (1) Use a **cash account** instead of a margin account -- cash accounts are exempt from PDT rules, but you must wait for trades to settle (T+1) before reusing funds. (2) Focus on swing trading or position trading instead of day trading. (3) If you want to day trade, ensure you maintain $25,000+ at all times.

> **⚠️ User 2 (India):** **SEBI margin requirements** govern how much capital you need for different types of trades. Key points: (1) **Peak margin reporting** -- brokers must block margins upfront before you can trade. You cannot use unrealized profits as margin for new trades intraday. (2) **Intraday leverage has been significantly reduced** by SEBI in recent years (previously brokers offered 10-20x leverage; now it is much tighter). (3) There is **no PDT equivalent** in India -- you can day trade as much as you want. (4) **F&O has minimum lot sizes** (e.g., Nifty 50 futures require a lot size of 25 units, meaning you need significant capital). SEBI has also increased minimum lot values for options. (5) **MTM (Mark to Market) losses** in F&O must be settled daily. Ensure you have sufficient funds in your trading account to avoid margin shortfalls and penalty charges.

### 3.3 Portfolio Diversification

**Diversification** means spreading your investments across different assets so that a loss in one area is offset by gains in another.

**"Don't put all your eggs in one basket."**

#### How to Diversify

1. **Across asset classes**: Mix stocks, bonds, ETFs, and possibly real estate or commodities.
2. **Across sectors**: Technology, healthcare, finance, energy, consumer goods, etc. Don't be 100% in tech.
3. **Across geographies**: U.S., international developed markets (Europe, Japan), emerging markets (India, Brazil).
4. **Across company sizes**: Large-cap, mid-cap, small-cap.
5. **Across strategies**: Some growth stocks, some value stocks, some dividend stocks.

#### Sample Beginner Portfolio Allocation (Long-Term)

| Asset | Allocation | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| U.S. Total Market ETF | 40% | VTI (Vanguard Total Stock Market) |
| International ETF | 20% | VXUS (Vanguard Total International) |
| U.S. Bond ETF | 20% | BND (Vanguard Total Bond Market) |
| Sector or Thematic ETF | 10% | QQQ (NASDAQ 100) or sector-specific |
| Individual stocks (optional) | 10% | Companies you believe in and have researched |

**Note**: This is a conservative, long-term allocation. Your allocation should depend on your age, risk tolerance, and financial goals. Younger investors can typically afford more stock exposure; older investors shift toward bonds.

#### The "Three-Fund Portfolio"

A popular simple strategy recommended by the Bogleheads community:

1. U.S. Total Stock Market Index Fund (e.g., VTI or VTSAX)
2. International Stock Market Index Fund (e.g., VXUS or VTIAX)
3. U.S. Total Bond Market Fund (e.g., BND or VBTLX)

This gives you broad, global diversification with extremely low fees.

### 3.4 Market Indicators

Indicators are mathematical calculations based on price, volume, or open interest that traders use to analyze charts and make decisions.

#### Moving Averages (MA)

**What**: A line on the chart showing the average price over a specific number of periods.

- **Simple Moving Average (SMA)**: The straight average of prices over N periods. Example: 50-day SMA = average of the last 50 closing prices.
- **Exponential Moving Average (EMA)**: Gives more weight to recent prices, making it more responsive to new information.

**Common periods**: 9, 20, 50, 100, 200 days.

**How to use:**

- Price above the 200-day MA = generally bullish (uptrend).
- Price below the 200-day MA = generally bearish (downtrend).
- **Golden Cross**: The 50-day MA crosses above the 200-day MA. Bullish signal.
- **Death Cross**: The 50-day MA crosses below the 200-day MA. Bearish signal.
- Moving averages can act as dynamic support and resistance levels.

#### RSI (Relative Strength Index)

**What**: A momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of price changes. Ranges from 0 to 100.

**How to read:**

- **Above 70**: The stock is considered "overbought" -- it may be due for a pullback or reversal.
- **Below 30**: The stock is considered "oversold" -- it may be due for a bounce or reversal.
- **50 line**: Can act as a trend indicator. RSI staying above 50 = bullish momentum; below 50 = bearish.

**Caution**: In strong trends, RSI can stay overbought or oversold for extended periods. Don't blindly buy or sell based on RSI alone.

#### MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

**What**: A trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two moving averages of a stock's price.

**Components:**

1. **MACD Line**: 12-day EMA minus 26-day EMA.
2. **Signal Line**: 9-day EMA of the MACD Line.
3. **Histogram**: Visual bar showing the difference between the MACD Line and Signal Line.

**How to read:**

- **MACD crosses above Signal Line**: Bullish signal (potential buy).
- **MACD crosses below Signal Line**: Bearish signal (potential sell).
- **Histogram growing**: Momentum is increasing.
- **Histogram shrinking**: Momentum is fading.
- **Divergence**: When price makes new highs but MACD does not (bearish divergence), or price makes new lows but MACD does not (bullish divergence). Divergences can signal potential reversals.

#### Volume

**What**: The number of shares traded during a given period.

**Why it matters:**

- Volume confirms trends. A price move on high volume is more significant than one on low volume.
- **Volume spike**: An unusually large increase in volume can signal the start of a new trend or a potential reversal.
- **Volume dry-up**: Decreasing volume during a trend may signal the trend is weakening.
- Average volume gives you context for what is "normal" for a stock.

#### Bollinger Bands

**What**: Three lines -- a 20-day SMA in the middle, with bands 2 standard deviations above and below.

**How to read:**

- Price touching the upper band: Stock may be overbought.
- Price touching the lower band: Stock may be oversold.
- **Squeeze**: When the bands narrow (low volatility), a big price move may be coming.
- **Expansion**: Widening bands indicate increasing volatility.

#### VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)

**What**: The average price a stock has traded at throughout the day, weighted by volume.

- Popular among day traders.
- Price above VWAP = bullish intraday bias.
- Price below VWAP = bearish intraday bias.
- Institutional traders often use VWAP as a benchmark for execution quality.

### 3.5 Bull vs. Bear Markets

#### Bull Market

- A sustained period of rising stock prices, generally defined as a 20% or greater increase from a recent low.
- Characterized by optimism, investor confidence, and economic growth.
- Bull markets historically last longer than bear markets (average of about 5-6 years).
- During bull markets, most stocks go up, which can make investors feel like geniuses. Be careful not to confuse a bull market with personal skill.

#### Bear Market

- A sustained period of falling stock prices, generally defined as a 20% or greater decline from a recent high.
- Characterized by pessimism, fear, and often economic downturns.
- Bear markets historically last about 9-16 months on average.
- Bear markets are where undisciplined traders lose the most money.
- However, bear markets also create buying opportunities for long-term investors.

#### Historical Context

- Since 1950, there have been roughly 10+ bear markets in the S&P 500.
- The market has recovered from every single one and gone on to new highs.
- "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful." -- Warren Buffett.

### 3.6 Market Sentiment

**Market sentiment** is the overall attitude or mood of investors toward the market or a particular security. It is often driven more by emotion than by fundamentals.

#### Sentiment Indicators

| Indicator | What It Measures |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **VIX (Volatility Index)** | Known as the "fear index." High VIX (above 30) = high fear. Low VIX (below 15) = complacency. Measures expected volatility in the S&P 500 over the next 30 days. |
| **Put/Call Ratio** | Ratio of put options to call options being traded. High ratio = bearish sentiment. Low ratio = bullish sentiment. |
| **AAII Sentiment Survey** | Weekly survey of individual investors: bullish, bearish, or neutral. Extreme readings are often contrarian indicators. |
| **CNN Fear & Greed Index** | Composite of 7 indicators on a scale of 0 (extreme fear) to 100 (extreme greed). Free to check at money.cnn.com. |
| **Social media / news tone** | What people are saying on Twitter/X, Reddit, financial news. |

#### Contrarian Thinking

- When everyone is extremely bullish, it often means most people who want to buy have already bought, and the market may be near a top.
- When everyone is extremely bearish, it often means most sellers have already sold, and the market may be near a bottom.
- Sentiment should be used as one input among many, not as a sole trading signal.

## 5. Common Mistakes Beginners Make

### 5.1 Emotional Trading

**The problem**: Letting fear, greed, excitement, or frustration drive your trading decisions instead of logic and your predetermined strategy.

**How it manifests:**

- **Panic selling**: The market drops 5% and you sell everything at a loss, only to watch it recover the next week.
- **Revenge trading**: You take a loss and immediately make another trade to "win it back," often taking on even more risk.
- **Euphoria**: You have a few winning trades and feel invincible. You increase your position sizes recklessly.
- **Anchoring**: You refuse to sell a losing stock because "it was at $100 last month" even though nothing about the fundamentals supports that price anymore.

**How to avoid it:**

- Have a written trading plan BEFORE entering any trade (entry price, stop-loss, profit target, position size).
- Walk away from the screen when you feel emotional.
- Accept that losses are a normal part of trading. Even the best traders lose 40-50% of their trades.
- Keep a trading journal and review it regularly to identify emotional patterns.

### 5.2 Over-Leveraging

**The problem**: Using borrowed money (margin) or leveraged products (options, leveraged ETFs) to amplify your bets beyond what your account can handle.

**How it manifests:**

- Buying stocks on margin when you barely understand how margin works.
- Buying far out-of-the-money options because they are "cheap" (they are cheap for a reason -- they almost always expire worthless).
- Using 3x leveraged ETFs (like TQQQ or SQQQ) as long-term holdings (they are designed for single-day holding only due to daily rebalancing and volatility decay).

**How to avoid it:**

- Start with a cash account (no margin).
- Do not trade options until you have at least 6-12 months of experience with stocks.
- If you use margin, never use more than 25% of your available margin.
- Understand that leverage amplifies losses just as much as it amplifies gains. A 50% loss requires a 100% gain just to break even.

### 5.3 Not Having a Strategy

**The problem**: Trading based on tips, gut feelings, or whatever stock is trending on social media, with no consistent approach or system.

**How it manifests:**

- Buying a stock because someone on Reddit said it's going "to the moon."
- Having no criteria for entry, exit, position size, or risk management.
- Constantly switching strategies after a few losing trades.
- Not knowing why you bought a stock or when you plan to sell.

**How to avoid it:**

- Develop a simple, written trading plan. It should answer:
  1. What is my strategy? (e.g., "I buy stocks near their 200-day moving average with strong earnings growth")
  2. What are my entry criteria?
  3. Where is my stop-loss?
  4. Where is my profit target?
  5. How much of my account will I risk per trade?
- Backtest your strategy (look at historical data to see if it would have worked).
- Stick to your strategy for at least 50-100 trades before evaluating whether it works.
- Track every trade in a journal.

### 5.4 FOMO Trading (Fear of Missing Out)

**The problem**: Buying a stock because it is already up significantly and you are afraid of missing further gains.

**How it manifests:**

- A stock jumps 30% in a day on social media hype. You buy at the top. It drops 25% the next day.
- You see others posting gains on a stock and feel left out, so you chase it even though your analysis doesn't support the entry.
- You buy after a stock has already had a huge run without waiting for a pullback or proper setup.

**How to avoid it:**

- Remember: there will ALWAYS be another opportunity. The market is not going anywhere. Missing one trade is not the end of the world.
- If a stock has already moved significantly, wait for a pullback before considering entry.
- Ask yourself: "Would I buy this stock if no one else was talking about it?"
- Have a watchlist prepared in advance. When a stock meets your criteria, you will feel less urgency to chase random opportunities.

### 5.5 Other Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Not using stop-losses** | Hoping a losing trade will "come back." This turns small losses into catastrophic ones. |
| **Overtrading** | Trading too frequently, racking up fees and making impulsive decisions. Quality over quantity. |
| **Ignoring fees and taxes** | Frequent trading generates short-term capital gains (taxed at higher rates) and potential wash sale issues. |
| **Averaging down blindly** | Buying more of a losing stock to lower your average cost without any analysis supporting the decision. Sometimes a stock is going down for good reason. |
| **Failing to diversify** | Putting all your money in one stock, one sector, or one asset class. One piece of bad news can devastate your account. |
| **Comparing yourself to others** | Social media shows wins, not losses. Comparing your returns to curated highlight reels leads to poor decisions. |
| **Ignoring the macro environment** | Not paying attention to interest rates, Federal Reserve decisions, inflation data, and broader economic trends that affect all stocks. |
| **Trading without education** | Jumping into real trading without studying. Would you perform surgery without going to medical school? Treat trading with the same seriousness. |
| **Trying to time the market perfectly** | No one consistently buys at the exact bottom and sells at the exact top. "Time in the market beats timing the market" is backed by decades of data. |
| **Following "gurus" blindly** | Many self-proclaimed trading gurus make more money selling courses than actually trading. Be skeptical of anyone promising guaranteed returns. |

### 5.6 User-Specific Common Mistakes

> **⚠️ User 1 (H1B/US) -- Mistakes to Watch For:**
>
> - **Not tracking FBAR/FATCA requirements:** Many H1B holders have Indian bank accounts, PPF, mutual funds, or fixed deposits that trigger FBAR ($10K aggregate) and FATCA ($50K+) filing requirements. Missing these filings carries severe penalties -- up to $10,000 per non-willful violation for FBAR. This is not optional.
> - **Not understanding H1B passive income rules:** Passive investment income (trading gains, dividends) is fine. But if you set up a trading LLC or treat trading as a business, immigration authorities could argue it constitutes unauthorized employment. Keep it personal, not business.
> - **Over-trading and triggering PDT restrictions:** Making 4+ day trades in 5 business days in a margin account without $25,000 equity will get your account restricted. This is a common trap for new traders who get excited. Track your day trades carefully or use a cash account.
> - **Ignoring state tax implications:** Moving between states (e.g., California to Texas) mid-year creates complex state tax situations. Some states tax capital gains at very high rates.
> - **Forgetting to update Indian accounts to NRI status:** If your Indian bank accounts are still in resident status, you are violating FEMA regulations. Convert to NRE/NRO accounts.

> **⚠️ User 2 (India) -- Mistakes to Watch For:**
>
> - **Not understanding TCS on LRS remittances:** When you send money abroad for investing via LRS, your bank will deduct **20% TCS on amounts above ₹7 lakh/year**. This is a significant cash flow impact. While you can claim it back as a credit when filing ITR, you need to plan for the upfront hit. Many new investors are shocked by this.
> - **Ignoring crypto tax obligations:** The **30% flat tax** on crypto gains is harsh but unavoidable. Many people trade crypto informally and do not report it -- this is illegal. Exchanges report to the tax department. The 1% TDS creates a paper trail. Do not ignore this.
> - **F&O tax audit triggers:** If your F&O turnover exceeds thresholds, you need a tax audit by a CA. Many beginners start trading F&O without realizing the compliance burden. Even a few trades can create turnover figures that approach audit thresholds when you look at total absolute profit/loss. Budget for CA fees.
> - **Not filing ITR because "I am unemployed":** Being unemployed does not exempt you from filing. If your trading income, interest income, or any other income exceeds ₹2.5 lakh (old regime) or ₹3 lakh (new regime), you must file. Even below the limit, filing is required if you hold foreign assets or want to carry forward losses.
> - **Not reporting foreign assets in Schedule FA:** If you invest in US stocks via LRS/Vested/INDmoney, you must declare these holdings in Schedule FA of your ITR. Non-disclosure can lead to penalties under the Black Money Act.
> - **Underestimating F&O risk as a beginner:** F&O is treated as business income, meaning losses can only be set off against business income. SEBI has been tightening regulations because retail F&O traders lose money at alarming rates (SEBI studies show ~90% of F&O retail traders incur losses).

## Quick-Start Checklist for Absolute Beginners

1. [ ] Read at least 3-5 articles on Investopedia to learn basic terminology.
2. [ ] Watch a beginner investing course on Khan Academy or YouTube.
3. [ ] Open a brokerage account (Fidelity or Schwab recommended).
4. [ ] Paper trade for at least 1-3 months to learn the platform and practice strategies.
5. [ ] Read at least one book from the "Must-Read" list (start with "The Little Book of Common Sense Investing" for the easiest read).
6. [ ] Write a simple trading/investing plan before putting real money in.
7. [ ] Start with a small amount you can afford to lose entirely ($100-$500).
8. [ ] Focus on ETFs or blue-chip stocks initially, not penny stocks or options.
9. [ ] Keep a trading journal from day one.
10. [ ] Set realistic expectations: you will NOT double your money in a month. Consistent 8-12% annual returns is excellent.

## Final Words

The stock market is one of the most powerful wealth-building tools in history, but it rewards patience, discipline, and continuous learning. The vast majority of people who lose money in the market do so because they skip the education phase, trade emotionally, or try to get rich quick.

The best advice for any beginner:

- **Start slow, stay humble, and never stop learning.**
- **Invest in yourself (education) before investing in the market.**
- **The goal is not to get rich overnight. The goal is to still be in the game 10 years from now.**
